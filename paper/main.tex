\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Realized volatility forecasting is central to risk management and derivative pricing, yet no study has systematically assessed whether pretrained time series foundation models (TSFMs) can match established econometric benchmarks for this task.
We evaluate eight zero-shot TSFMs against six econometric specifications on 50 assets from the VOLARE dataset (equities, FX, futures; 2015--2026) at horizons of 1, 5, and 22 days, using Diebold--Mariano tests and Model Confidence Sets.
The best TSFMs outperform the best econometric benchmarks across all horizons and asset classes. Sundial, a flow-matching generative model, achieves the lowest quasi-likelihood (QLIKE) loss at every horizon and enters the Model Confidence Set for 97.5--100\% of equities; its advantage over the best econometric specification (Log-HAR) grows from 11\% at $h = 1$ to 37\% at $h = 22$. However, performance is highly heterogeneous across architectures: some models produce severely miscalibrated forecasts, and model selection within the TSFM class matters as much as the choice between TSFMs and econometric benchmarks.
These results suggest that practitioners can deploy zero-shot TSFMs as a drop-in replacement for HAR-family models, provided they evaluate multiple architectures rather than adopting a single TSFM by default.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Realized volatility, heterogeneous autoregressive model, time series foundation models, zero-shot forecasting

\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} C22, C53, C55, G17
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================

Forecasting realized volatility is central to risk management, derivative pricing, and portfolio allocation. Since the work of \citet{andersen1998} and \citet{barndorffnielsen2002}, realized volatility constructed from high-frequency returns has become the standard model-free measure of ex-post price variation. The Heterogeneous Autoregressive (HAR) model of \citet{corsi2009} is the dominant forecasting benchmark: its three-component structure, aggregating past realized volatility at daily, weekly, and monthly frequencies, provides a parsimonious approximation to the long-memory dynamics that characterize volatility. Extensions incorporating jump variation \citep{andersen2007roughing}, semivariance asymmetries \citep{patton2015}, and measurement error corrections \citep{bollerslev2016harq} have yielded mixed practical gains \citep{clements2021practical, branco2024}. Machine learning methods, including neural networks \citep{bucci2020, brini2025spotv2net}, random forests \citep{luong2018}, graph-based approaches \citep{zhang2024intraday, zhang2025graph}, and convolutional architectures \citep{morenopino2024deepvol}, have also been applied, but a broader assessment by \citet{christensen2023ml} concludes that machine learning offers incremental improvements rather than a paradigm shift.

A different class of models has emerged over the past two years: time series foundation models (TSFMs). These are large pretrained transformer neural networks \citep{vaswani2017}---architectures that learn dependencies between positions in a sequence through an attention mechanism, without imposing a fixed lag structure---trained on billions of time series observations across domains, capable of producing forecasts for previously unseen series without any retraining, a capability known as zero-shot forecasting. Leading examples include Chronos \citep{ansari2024chronos}, Moirai \citep{woo2024moirai}, and Lag-Llama \citep{rasul2024lagllama}, several of which have released second-generation versions with improved architectures \citep{ansari2025chronos2, liu2025moirai2}. Surveys document the rapid pace of development in this area \citep{liang2024tsfmsurvey, ye2024tsfmsurvey, miller2024tsfmsurvey}. Outside finance, \citet{carriero2024macro} applied zero-shot language models to macroeconomic forecasting and found that they do not outperform traditional econometric baselines, raising the question of whether similar conclusions hold for financial time series.

Applications of TSFMs to financial time series remain limited. \citet{goel2025rv} tested TimesFM~1.0 on realized volatility for 20 stocks and found that fine-tuning was necessary for the model to compete with HAR; zero-shot performance was poor. However, that negative result may be model-vintage-specific rather than a fundamental limitation of zero-shot inference: TimesFM~2.5, released one year later, performs well zero-shot in our evaluation. \citet{rahimikia2025revisiting} evaluated several TSFMs on daily stock returns and reported uniformly negative zero-shot results. Realized volatility differs from returns in ways that may favor foundation models: it is strictly positive, exhibits well-documented mean reversion and long-memory dependence \citep{andersen2003}, and follows a distribution closer to the macroeconomic and physical series on which TSFMs were pretrained.

To our knowledge, no study has yet evaluated multiple foundation models on realized volatility with formal statistical testing. This paper conducts the first systematic comparison of zero-shot TSFMs against established econometric benchmarks for realized volatility forecasting. We evaluate eight TSFMs---spanning six distinct architectures---against six econometric specifications across 50 assets spanning three asset classes (equities, FX, futures) and three forecast horizons ($h = 1, 5, 22$ days), using the recently released VOLARE dataset \citep{volare2026}. The zero-shot setting, with no domain-specific training, isolates the value of general time series pretraining. We apply Diebold--Mariano (DM) tests \citep{diebold1995} and Model Confidence Set (MCS) analysis \citep{hansen2011mcs} to control for multiple comparisons.

The best zero-shot TSFMs outperform the best econometric benchmarks across all horizons and asset classes, contrasting with prior negative findings on daily returns and on TimesFM~1.0. Sundial, a flow-matching generative model, achieves the lowest QLIKE at every horizon and enters the MCS for 97.5--100\% of equities. The TSFM advantage grows with forecast horizon, reaching its largest margin at the monthly horizon. However, performance is highly heterogeneous across TSFM architectures: some models produce severely miscalibrated forecasts while others outperform all benchmarks, indicating that model selection within the TSFM class is as important as the choice between TSFMs and econometric models.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:literature} reviews the related literature. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the VOLARE dataset. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} presents the econometric and foundation model specifications, along with the forecast evaluation framework. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the empirical results on univariate realized variance. Section~\ref{sec:significance} assesses statistical significance through DM tests, MCS, Mincer--Zarnowitz regressions, and Giacomini--Rossi fluctuation tests. Section~\ref{sec:robustness} contains robustness checks, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Realized Volatility Modeling}
\label{subsec:rv_modeling}

The theory of realized volatility originates with \citet{andersen1998} and \citet{andersen2001exchange}, who showed that the sum of squared intraday returns provides a consistent, nonparametric estimator of the integrated variance of asset prices. \citet{barndorffnielsen2002} formalized this result in a semimartingale framework, establishing that realized variance converges to integrated variance as the sampling frequency increases. Subsequent work identified the key stylized facts of realized volatility: approximate log-normality, long-memory dependence with a fractional integration parameter $d \approx 0.4$, and slow mean reversion \citep{andersen2003}.

At ultra-high sampling frequencies, microstructure noise from bid-ask bounce and discrete price changes biases the realized variance estimator upward. \citet{barndorffnielsen2008} introduced the realized kernel as a noise-consistent alternative that remains valid in the presence of market microstructure effects.

The HAR model of \citet{corsi2009} became the standard forecasting benchmark for realized volatility. By including daily, weekly, and monthly RV components as regressors, the model approximates long-memory behavior through a cascade of time scales corresponding to heterogeneous trading horizons. The specification is parsimonious (three regressors plus a constant) yet achieves accuracy comparable to far more complex alternatives.

A large body of work has extended the HAR framework. The HAR-J model \citep{andersen2007roughing} separates realized variance into continuous and jump components using bipower variation \citep{barndorffnielsen2004bipower}. The HAR-RS model \citep{patton2015, barndorffnielsen2010semivariance} decomposes realized variance into positive and negative semivariance to capture asymmetric responses to upside and downside moves. The HARQ model \citep{bollerslev2016harq} interacts the daily RV regressor with realized quarticity to account for time-varying measurement error. \citet{clements2021practical} provides a practical guide to implementing and comparing these extensions.

Long-memory models offer a complementary approach. Autoregressive Fractionally Integrated Moving Average (ARFIMA) models \citep{baillie1996figarch, izzeldin2019} explicitly parameterize the fractional integration order and remain competitive at longer forecast horizons where the slow mean reversion of realized volatility becomes the dominant dynamic.
% REVIEW NOTE (Comment 7): Removed rough volatility discussion (Gatheral 2018, Bennedsen 2022). The rough volatility framework concerns the Hurst exponent of the volatility process path, which is tangential to our forecasting comparison and does not directly inform any model we test.

Machine learning methods have been applied to realized volatility forecasting with mixed results. \citet{bucci2020} reports that feedforward and recurrent neural networks outperform HAR on a sample of U.S.\ stocks. \citet{luong2018} finds gains from random forests. \citet{morenopino2024deepvol} introduces DeepVol, a convolutional neural network that processes raw order book data. In contrast, \citet{branco2024} concludes that simple linear models are difficult to beat after correcting for multiple testing, and \citet{christensen2023ml} finds that machine learning offers only incremental improvements over standard econometric methods. Recent surveys by \citet{gunnarsson2024} and \citet{leushuis2026} cover this literature.

\subsection{Time Series Foundation Models}
\label{subsec:tsfm}

Time series foundation models are large pretrained models that produce forecasts for arbitrary time series without task-specific training, analogous to large language models for text \citep{liang2024tsfmsurvey}. TSFMs differ from task-specific deep learning models (neural networks, recurrent networks, convolutional architectures) in a fundamental way: deep learning models must be trained from scratch on the target series before they can produce any forecast, while TSFMs are pretrained on large external corpora and can forecast immediately via zero-shot inference. This eliminates the need for domain-specific training data, hyperparameter tuning, and GPU-intensive estimation---a practical advantage when deploying to new assets or markets where historical data may be limited. TSFMs are trained on vast corpora of time series drawn from diverse domains (weather, energy, retail, transport, macroeconomics) and learn general temporal patterns that transfer to unseen series at inference time.

Three architectural families dominate the first generation of TSFMs. Chronos \citep{ansari2024chronos} converts continuous values into discrete tokens via quantile binning and uses a T5 encoder-decoder architecture for autoregressive forecasting. T5, the Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer \citep{raffel2020}, is a sequence-to-sequence model that reads the full input through an encoder and generates output tokens autoregressively through a decoder. Moirai \citep{woo2024moirai} handles arbitrary numbers of variates through an ``any-variate'' attention mechanism and uses mixture distribution outputs for uncertainty quantification. Lag-Llama \citep{rasul2024lagllama} adapts the LLaMA decoder-only architecture for probabilistic time series forecasting using lag-based tokenization. Finance-specific foundation models have also been proposed, including Kronos \citep{shi2025kronos}, which is pretrained on candlestick (open-high-low-close-volume, OHLCV) data from over 45 global exchanges using a learned tokenizer that maps price patterns into discrete tokens.\footnote{We do not evaluate Kronos because it expects OHLCV price data as input and cannot be meaningfully applied to univariate realized variance without constructing synthetic candlestick data, which introduces an arbitrary input transformation unrelated to the model's intended use case.}

Second-generation models appeared in 2025. \citet{ansari2025chronos2} introduced Chronos-2 and Chronos-Bolt: the former adds multivariate support and covariate handling, while Chronos-Bolt is a distilled variant, meaning its parameters were trained to approximate the full Chronos-2 model's behavior at a fraction of the computational cost, producing direct quantile forecasts in a single forward pass and running up to 250 times faster than the original. Moirai~2.0 \citep{liu2025moirai2} demonstrated that smaller, better-trained models can match or exceed their larger predecessors using multi-token prediction and improved tokenization. Other models include TimesFM \citep{das2024timesfm}, a patch-based decoder model from Google; Toto \citep{cohen2024toto}, an observability-focused model from Datadog; Moirai-MoE \citep{liu2024moiraimoe}, a sparse Mixture of Experts extension of Moirai; Sundial \citep{liu2025sundial}, which uses flow matching for generative forecasting; MOMENT \citep{goswami2024moment}, a masked-encoder architecture designed for multiple time series tasks (forecasting, classification, anomaly detection); and Timer \citep{liu2024timer}.

Standardized benchmarks have been developed to evaluate TSFMs across domains. GIFT-Eval \citep{aksu2024gifteval} provides a unified evaluation protocol across multiple datasets and forecast horizons. FEV-Bench \citep{shchur2025fevbench} focuses on forecasting evaluation with emphasis on calibration. TSFM-Bench \citep{li2024tsfmbench} compares models across zero-shot and fine-tuned settings. A consistent finding is that no single model dominates across all domains and horizons, which motivates domain-specific evaluations such as the one we undertake here. \citet{adler2025calibration} raises additional concerns about the calibration of TSFM prediction intervals, finding systematic miscoverage across several models.

\subsection{Foundation Models for Volatility Forecasting}
\label{subsec:fm_volatility}

The application of TSFMs to volatility forecasting is recent and limited. The closest prior work is \citet{goel2025rv}, who tested TimesFM~1.0 on realized volatility for 20 stocks. They found that zero-shot TimesFM underperformed HAR and that fine-tuning was necessary to achieve competitive accuracy. Our paper differs in several respects: we evaluate eight foundation models spanning six distinct architectures (Chronos-Bolt, Moirai~2.0 and Moirai-MoE, Lag-Llama, TimesFM~2.5, Toto, and Sundial), use newer model generations, and apply MCS analysis to control for multiple comparisons. The same research group also applied TimesFM to Value-at-Risk forecasting \citep{goel2024var}.

\citet{rahimikia2025revisiting} evaluated Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, and MOMENT on daily stock returns and found that zero-shot TSFMs consistently underperformed ARIMA. However, daily returns differ from realized volatility in fundamental ways: returns are approximately symmetric, near-white-noise, and lack the strong serial dependence that characterizes realized volatility. Realized volatility is strictly positive, mean-reverting, and displays long-memory dynamics, properties that bring it closer to the macroeconomic and physical time series on which TSFMs were pretrained. This distinction suggests that negative results on returns need not carry over to realized measures.

Other financial applications include stock price forecasting \citep{laniewski2025chronos, valeyre2024chronosstocks}, foreign exchange volatility modeling \citep{nguyen2025volabert}, and multivariate financial time series \citep{marconi2025}. Finance-specific foundation models have also been proposed, including FinCast \citep{zhu2025fincast} and Kronos \citep{shi2025kronos}.

The question of whether to fine-tune or apply TSFMs zero-shot is itself an active area of research. \citet{das2025icf} explored in-context fine-tuning for TimesFM, while \citet{germanmorales2024lora} and \citet{gupta2024beyondlora} investigated parameter-efficient adaptation via Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) \citep{hu2022lora}. Testing zero-shot performance first establishes an important baseline: if a TSFM already performs well without adaptation, the marginal value of fine-tuning may be limited, and the zero-shot workflow offers practical advantages in simplicity and computational cost.

The gap in this literature---the absence of a multi-model, multi-asset evaluation with formal statistical testing for realized volatility---motivates the empirical design we describe next.

\section{The VOLARE Dataset}
\label{sec:data}
% ============================================================

% REVIEW NOTE (Comment 23): Merged the two data subsections into one section to reduce unnecessary gaps.

We use the VOLARE (VOLatility Archive for Realized Estimates) dataset of \citet{volare2026}, which provides daily realized volatility measures for a broad cross-section of financial assets. VOLARE is constructed from ultra-high-frequency tick data sourced from Kibot, covering 40 U.S.\ equities, 5 major currency pairs, and 5 commodity and index futures contracts. The equity sample begins on January 2, 2015 and runs through January 30, 2026, yielding 2,786 trading days per stock. The FX and futures samples begin on September 25, 2009 (4,242 and 4,228 trading days, respectively).

For each asset-day, VOLARE provides realized measures computed at multiple sampling frequencies. All measures used in this paper are computed at the 5-minute sampling frequency: realized variance ($RV$), bipower variation ($BPV$), positive and negative realized semivariance ($RS^{+}$, $RS^{-}$), and realized quarticity ($RQ$). All values are expressed in decimal squared returns; for instance, a typical daily $RV$ for a U.S.\ equity is approximately $1.9 \times 10^{-4}$, corresponding to an annualized volatility of roughly 22\%. VOLARE is well suited for our multi-asset comparison because it constructs all realized measures using a uniform methodology across assets, ensuring that cross-asset differences in model rankings reflect genuine forecasting performance rather than measurement inconsistencies. The sample period (2015--2026 for equities, 2009--2026 for FX and futures) spans multiple volatility regimes, including the low-volatility period of 2017--2019, the COVID-19 shock of 2020, and the subsequent recovery, providing a sufficiently rich evaluation environment.
Our equity sample comprises the 40 stocks in VOLARE that span the 2015--2026 period (approximately 2,786 trading days): AAPL, ADBE, AMD, AMGN, AMZN, AXP, BA, CAT, CRM, CSCO, CVX, DIS, GE, GOOGL, GS, HD, HON, IBM, JNJ, JPM, KO, MCD, META, MMM, MRK, MSFT, NFLX, NKE, NVDA, ORCL, PG, PM, SHW, TRV, TSLA, UNH, V, VZ, WMT, and XOM. These cover all 11 Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) sectors. The FX sample consists of five major currency pairs (AUDUSD, EURUSD, GBPUSD, USDCAD, USDJPY) and the futures sample covers five contracts (Corn, Crude Oil, E-mini S\&P 500, Gold, Natural Gas). Both FX and futures samples span 2009--2026 (approximately 4,240 trading days), providing substantially longer histories than the equity sample because VOLARE's intraday coverage for these asset classes begins earlier.

Table~\ref{tab:descriptive} reports descriptive statistics for the 5-minute realized variance across the three asset classes. As expected for a strictly positive quantity, realized variance is right-skewed across all asset classes, with heavy tails (kurtosis ranging from 35 to over 3,000) confirming that extreme volatility episodes are a persistent feature of the data. The first-order autocorrelation of daily RV, denoted $\rho_1$, averages 0.606 for equities, consistent with the well-documented persistence of volatility; the 22-day autocorrelation ($\rho_{22}$, i.e., the sample autocorrelation at lag 22) averages 0.085, indicating slow decay. The FX pairs display RV approximately two orders of magnitude smaller than equities (cross-sectional average $\bar{RV} \approx 3 \times 10^{-5}$ vs.\ $2 \times 10^{-4}$), with comparable persistence (cross-sectional average $\bar{\rho}_1 = 0.52$, where the bar denotes averaging across assets). \section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ============================================================

This section describes the fourteen forecasting models in our comparison: six econometric benchmarks (Section~\ref{subsec:benchmarks}), eight TSFMs (Section~\ref{subsec:tsfm_method}), and the evaluation framework (Section~\ref{subsec:evaluation}).

\subsection{Econometric Benchmarks}
\label{subsec:benchmarks}

We consider six econometric specifications that span the main approaches to realized volatility forecasting: the HAR family and its extensions, which exploit multi-scale aggregation, and ARFIMA, which explicitly models long memory.

\paragraph{HAR Model \citep{corsi2009}.}
The HAR model captures the multi-scale persistence of realized volatility by aggregating past RV at daily, weekly, and monthly frequencies:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 RV_t + \beta_2 RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 RV_{t-22:t} + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:har}
\end{equation}
where $RV_{t-k:t} \equiv k^{-1}\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} RV_{t-i}$ denotes the average realized volatility over the previous $k$ days. We use the original specification, where the weekly and monthly components include overlapping lags (i.e., the weekly component averages days $t$ through $t-4$, not the non-overlapping ``rotated'' version that separates lags 2--5 from 6--22). This three-component structure approximates long-memory behavior through the actions of heterogeneous market participants operating at different time horizons.

\paragraph{HAR-J Model \citep{andersen2007roughing}.}
The HAR-J model augments the baseline HAR with a jump component to separate continuous and discontinuous variation:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 RV_t + \beta_2 RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 RV_{t-22:t} + \beta_J J_t + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:har_j}
\end{equation}
where $J_t = \max(RV_t - BPV_t,\, 0)$ measures the jump component as the positive part of the difference between realized variance and bipower variation ($BPV$), a jump-robust estimator of integrated variance constructed from products of adjacent absolute returns \citep{barndorffnielsen2004bipower}. A negative coefficient on $J_t$ indicates that large jumps reduce, rather than increase, future volatility.

\paragraph{HAR-RS Model \citep{patton2015}.}
The HAR-RS model decomposes realized variance into positive and negative semivariance components to capture asymmetric volatility responses:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1^+ RS_t^+ + \beta_1^- RS_t^- + \beta_2^+ RS_{t-5:t}^+ + \beta_2^- RS_{t-5:t}^- + \beta_3^+ RS_{t-22:t}^+ + \beta_3^- RS_{t-22:t}^- + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:har_rs}
\end{equation}
where $RS_t^+$ and $RS_t^-$ denote good and bad realized semivariance, respectively, and $RS_t^+ + RS_t^- = RV_t$ by construction \citep{barndorffnielsen2010semivariance}. The six-regressor structure allows upside and downside risk to follow separate dynamics at each aggregation frequency.

\paragraph{HARQ Model \citep{bollerslev2016harq}.}
The HARQ model accounts for time-varying measurement error in realized volatility by interacting the daily RV regressor with the square root of realized quarticity:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + (\beta_1 + \beta_1^Q \sqrt{RQ_t})\, RV_t + \beta_2 RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 RV_{t-22:t} + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:harq}
\end{equation}
where $RQ_t$ is the realized quarticity. The interaction term attenuates the daily RV signal when measurement noise is high, as indicated by large values of $RQ_t$.

\paragraph{Log-HAR Model \citep{corsi2009}.}
The Log-HAR model applies the HAR specification to log-transformed realized volatility:
\begin{equation}
  \log RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \log RV_t + \beta_2 \log RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 \log RV_{t-22:t} + \varepsilon_{t+h}.
  \label{eq:log_har}
\end{equation}
The logarithmic transformation brings the RV distribution closer to normality, improving ordinary least squares (OLS) efficiency. Point forecasts in levels are recovered via bias-corrected retransformation: $\widehat{RV}_{t+h} = \exp(\hat{y}_{t+h} + \hat{\sigma}^2/2)$, where $\hat{\sigma}^2$ is the estimated residual variance, following the standard log-normal adjustment.

\paragraph{ARFIMA Model \citep{andersen2003}.}
The ARFIMA model captures the well-documented long-memory property of realized volatility through fractional differencing:
\begin{equation}
  (1 - L)^d\, \Phi(L)\,(\log RV_t - \mu) = \Theta(L)\,\varepsilon_t,
  \label{eq:arfima}
\end{equation}
where $d \in (0, 0.5)$ is the fractional integration parameter governing the rate at which autocorrelations decay (values near 0.4, typical for RV, imply slow hyperbolic decay rather than the exponential decay of standard ARMA models), $\Phi(L)$ and $\Theta(L)$ are autoregressive and moving average lag polynomials of orders $p$ and $q$ (with $p, q \leq 2$ selected by the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)), and $L$ is the lag operator. The parameter $d$ is estimated by maximum likelihood. The fractional differencing filter is truncated at $K = 100$ lags.

All HAR-family models are estimated by OLS with Newey--West heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent standard errors. The ARFIMA model is re-estimated every 22 trading days to balance computational cost against parameter staleness. We do not include the Realized GARCH model of \citet{hansen2012}, which jointly models daily returns and realized measures via maximum likelihood. VOLARE provides realized measures but not the daily close-to-close returns required for the return equation, and sourcing returns from an external provider would introduce alignment issues between the two data sources. This omission is unlikely to affect our main conclusions: \citet{hansen2012} report that Realized GARCH improves upon standard GARCH but does not consistently dominate HAR-family models in out-of-sample forecasting.

\subsection{Time Series Foundation Models}
\label{subsec:tsfm_method}

We evaluate eight time series foundation models (TSFMs) in a zero-shot setting, applied directly to realized volatility series without any domain-specific training or fine-tuning. The eight models span six distinct architectures: Chronos-Bolt (small and base checkpoints), Moirai~2.0, Lag-Llama, TimesFM~2.5, Toto, Sundial, and Moirai-MoE. The zero-shot approach is the most practically relevant deployment mode: it requires no labeled financial data, no model retraining, and can be applied immediately to any new asset. Among the TSFMs we evaluate, Chronos-Bolt, Moirai~2.0, and Sundial are inference-only architectures with no public fine-tuning API. Lag-Llama supports fine-tuning through its GluonTS estimator interface, and the full Chronos-2 model (distinct from the distilled Bolt variant) provides built-in LoRA fine-tuning via its \texttt{fit()} method; we leave these adaptation strategies for future work.\footnote{We do not evaluate MOMENT \citep{goswami2024moment} because it is pretrained with a masked reconstruction objective rather than an autoregressive forecasting objective. Its forecasting head, a linear projection layer that maps patch embeddings to the forecast horizon, is not pretrained and must be trained on the target series before the model can produce any predictions \citep[Section~4.2]{goswami2024moment}. This makes it incompatible with our zero-shot evaluation protocol.}\footnote{\citet{carriero2024macro} additionally evaluate Tiny Time Mixers (TTM) \citep{ekambaram2024ttm} and TimeGPT \citep{garza2024timegpt} for macroeconomic forecasting. We exclude TTM due to software version conflicts with our inference environment and TimeGPT because it is a proprietary, closed-source API that does not permit inspection of model weights or training data, precluding reproducibility.}

\paragraph{Formal framework.}
A TSFM can be expressed as a parametric mapping from an observed history to a forecast distribution. Let $y_{1:T} = (y_1, \ldots, y_T)$ denote the context window of $T$ past observations (in our case, $T = 512$ daily RV values). A TSFM with pretrained parameters $\hat{\theta}$ produces a forecast of the next $H$ values:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{y}_{T+1:T+H} = f_{\hat{\theta}}(y_{1:T}),
  \label{eq:tsfm}
\end{equation}
where $f_{\hat{\theta}}$ maps the input sequence to a predictive distribution over future values.\footnote{More precisely, $f_{\hat{\theta}}$ outputs a distribution $\hat{p}(y_{T+1:T+H} \mid y_{1:T}; \hat{\theta})$ from which we extract the median as the point forecast. The parameters $\hat{\theta}$ are fixed at their pretrained values throughout our evaluation; no gradient updates occur during inference.} The parameters $\hat{\theta}$ are learned during a pretraining phase on large external corpora of time series spanning weather, energy, retail, transport, and macroeconomic domains. At inference time, the model receives only the context window $y_{1:T}$ and produces forecasts without any task-specific parameter updates---this is the zero-shot setting.

\paragraph{TSFM components.}
Every TSFM implements the mapping in Equation~\eqref{eq:tsfm} through three stages: \emph{tokenization}, \emph{encoding}, and \emph{decoding}. First, the raw series $y_{1:T}$ is converted into a sequence of discrete or continuous \emph{tokens}---the model's internal representation of the data. Tokenization serves the same role as variable construction in an econometric model: it determines which features of the history the model can ``see.'' The TSFMs we evaluate use different tokenization strategies (quantile binning, patch projection, lag features, flow matching), summarized in Table~\ref{tab:tsfm_summary} and described individually below. Second, the token sequence is processed by a \emph{transformer} \citep{vaswani2017}, a neural network in which each position attends to other positions via learned attention weights, capturing dependencies at arbitrary lags without imposing the specific functional form that HAR prescribes (daily, weekly, monthly components). Transformers come in two main variants: \emph{encoder-only} models, which read the entire input at once and produce all outputs simultaneously (analogous to a regression that conditions on the full history), and \emph{decoder-only} models, which process the sequence left-to-right and generate outputs one step at a time (analogous to an autoregressive model). Third, the transformer's output is passed through a \emph{decoding head} that maps internal representations back to forecasts in the original scale. Decoding heads differ across models: some output quantiles of the predictive distribution directly, others parameterize an explicit probability distribution (e.g., Student-$t$) from which forecast trajectories are sampled, and one (Sundial) generates forecast trajectories via learned continuous normalizing flows. In all cases, we extract the median as the point forecast, analogous to reporting the conditional median from a quantile regression.

\paragraph{\textbf{Chronos-Bolt} \citep{ansari2025chronos2}.}
Chronos-Bolt is a distilled variant of the Chronos family. The original Chronos model tokenizes continuous values by mapping them into 4,096 discrete bins via quantile binning, where each observation is replaced by the index of its nearest quantile in a reference distribution, much as one would discretize a continuous variable into histogram bins. The model then processes these token indices through a T5 encoder-decoder transformer and generates forecasts autoregressively, predicting one token at a time. Chronos-Bolt replaces this two-step architecture with an encoder-only transformer that reads the entire input context and produces all forecast quantiles in a single forward pass, without iterative decoding. This yields a 250$\times$ speedup over the original while maintaining competitive accuracy. The decoding head outputs quantile forecasts directly (e.g., the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles), bypassing the need to sample from a token distribution. We use both the small (48M parameters) and base (205M parameters) checkpoints\footnote{\texttt{amazon/chronos-bolt-small} and \texttt{amazon/chronos-bolt-base} on HuggingFace.} and take the median (0.5 quantile) as the point forecast.

\paragraph{\textbf{Moirai~2.0} \citep{liu2025moirai2}.}
Moirai~2.0 builds on the original Moirai architecture. It tokenizes the input series by dividing it into fixed-length \emph{patches} (contiguous blocks of consecutive observations, e.g., 32 days each) and projects each patch into the transformer's embedding space via a learned linear map. This is analogous to constructing non-overlapping moving-window summaries of the series, except that the summary is learned rather than hand-specified. The transformer is decoder-only: it processes patches causally (each patch attends only to itself and earlier patches), preserving the temporal ordering. Moirai~2.0 uses \emph{multi-token prediction}, generating multiple future patches in a single pass rather than one step at a time. The decoding head outputs nine quantile levels (0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95) per forecast step. We use the small checkpoint\footnote{\texttt{Salesforce/moirai-2.0-R-small} on HuggingFace.} and extract the median quantile as the point forecast.

\paragraph{\textbf{Lag-Llama} \citep{rasul2024lagllama}.}
Lag-Llama is a decoder-only foundation model based on the LLaMA architecture, adapted for univariate probabilistic time series forecasting. Unlike the patch-based models above, Lag-Llama tokenizes each time step individually using a set of \emph{lag features}: at each position $t$, the input vector consists of the current value and a fixed set of lagged values $(y_{t-1}, y_{t-2}, y_{t-7}, y_{t-14}, \ldots)$, concatenated with time-related covariates. This lag-based design is conceptually closer to an autoregressive econometric specification, since the model explicitly receives lagged values as inputs, though the transformer learns the mapping from lags to forecasts nonparametrically rather than through a linear projection. The decoding head parameterizes a Student-$t$ mixture distribution at each forecast step: it outputs the location, scale, and degrees-of-freedom parameters for each mixture component. Forecasts are generated by sampling trajectories from this distribution and taking the median across samples. We use the pretrained checkpoint (8 layers, 4 attention heads, 36-dimensional embeddings per head), sample 20 trajectories, and report the median as the point forecast.

\paragraph{\textbf{TimesFM~2.5} \citep{das2024timesfm}.}
TimesFM~2.5 is Google's decoder-only foundation model for time series forecasting. It uses a patch-based tokenization with residual connections: the input series is divided into fixed-length patches, and each patch embedding includes a residual connection to the raw input values. The model has 200M parameters and outputs 11 quantile levels (0.05, 0.1, \ldots, 0.9, 0.95) per forecast step. TimesFM was pretrained on a mixture of Google Trends data and public time series corpora. We use the \texttt{google/timesfm-2.5-200m-pytorch} checkpoint and extract the median quantile as the point forecast.

\paragraph{\textbf{Toto} \citep{cohen2024toto}.}
Toto is Datadog's decoder-only foundation model, designed for observability and infrastructure time series. It has 162M parameters and uses patch-based tokenization with a learned linear projection. The decoding head parameterizes a Student-$t$ mixture distribution, outputting location, scale, and degrees-of-freedom parameters for each mixture component. Toto was pretrained primarily on observability metrics (CPU usage, request latency, error rates), making it the only TSFM in our study whose training domain is furthest from financial time series. We use the \texttt{Datadog/Toto-Open-Base-1.0} checkpoint.

\paragraph{\textbf{Sundial} \citep{liu2025sundial}.}
Sundial is a generative foundation model from THUML (Tsinghua University) that uses flow matching rather than quantile regression for probabilistic forecasting. Flow matching learns a continuous transformation from a simple noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian) to the forecast distribution, analogous to how a copula maps uniform marginals to a joint distribution but operating in the time-series trajectory space. The model has 128M parameters and generates forecast trajectories by applying this learned transformation. This generative approach produces full distributional forecasts without discretizing the output space into quantile bins. We use the \texttt{thuml/sundial-base-128m} checkpoint, sample 20 trajectories, and report the median as the point forecast.

\paragraph{\textbf{Moirai-MoE} \citep{liu2024moiraimoe}.}
Moirai-MoE extends the Moirai architecture with a sparse Mixture of Experts (MoE) layer that routes each input patch to a subset of specialized expert networks. This allows the model to increase capacity without proportionally increasing inference cost: only a fraction of parameters are active for each input. The small checkpoint has 43M total parameters. Like Moirai~2.0, it uses patch-based tokenization and outputs 9 quantile levels. We use the \texttt{Salesforce/moirai-moe-1.0-R-small} checkpoint and extract the median quantile as the point forecast.

\paragraph{Zero-shot protocol.}
All eight TSFMs receive a rolling context window of 512 daily RV observations as input, with no additional features or covariates. The 512-day context length is the maximum supported by all architectures' positional encodings and is the default recommended setting.\footnote{The 512-day default is common across TSFMs for several reasons: it is a power of two ($2^9$), which aligns efficiently with GPU memory and yields clean patch counts (e.g., $512 / 16 = 32$ patches); learned positional encodings are initialized at a fixed maximum length during training, making extrapolation beyond that length unreliable; and 512 is long enough to capture long-memory dynamics yet short enough to accommodate most series in heterogeneous pretraining corpora without excessive padding. Some architectures support longer contexts (Chronos-Bolt up to 2,048), but 512 remains the most heavily validated setting.} It corresponds to approximately two years of trading data, providing enough history to capture the long-memory dynamics of realized volatility. By contrast, the 252-day rolling estimation window for econometric models follows the convention of using one year of daily data, which is standard in the HAR literature. The one-year window balances the need for sufficient data to estimate HAR's four parameters reliably against the risk of including stale observations from a different volatility regime. This reflects each approach's recommended configuration rather than an equal-information comparison; we view this as the practically relevant setup, since a practitioner would deploy each model with its standard settings. We assess sensitivity to the context window length in Section~\ref{sec:robustness}.

For multi-step horizons ($h > 1$), the point forecast is the average of the $h$ individual-step median forecasts. No parameters are updated during the out-of-sample evaluation period; all forecasts are purely zero-shot. We extract the median rather than the mean of each model's predictive distribution because the median is the optimal point forecast under MAE loss and is more robust to the right-skewed forecast distributions that arise in volatility applications; for symmetric distributions, the two coincide. We acknowledge that the median is not the optimal point forecast under QLIKE loss (the conditional mean is); however, in practice, the median is the standard output of probabilistic TSFMs and produces more stable forecasts than the sample mean when the predictive distribution is heavy-tailed. Table~\ref{tab:computational_cost} reports wall-clock timing for all models. Econometric specifications require $< 0.01$\,s per forecast step, run entirely on CPU, and impose no special hardware requirements; they execute on commodity hardware including a standard laptop. TSFM inference was conducted on the Duke Computing Cluster (DCC) using a single NVIDIA RTX A5000 GPU (24\,GB VRAM), with per-step times ranging from 0.014\,s (Chronos-Bolt-Small) to 0.084\,s (Lag-Llama). Even the slowest TSFM produces a forecast in under 0.1\,s per asset, making the per-step cost negligible relative to the estimation overhead that econometric models avoid entirely in the zero-shot setting.

\subsection{Pretraining Data and Contamination Risk}
\label{subsec:contamination}

A natural concern with zero-shot evaluation is whether TSFM performance is inflated because the models' pretraining corpora overlap with the test series. Table~\ref{tab:pretraining_data} summarizes each model's training data and its financial content. No TSFM in our study was trained on realized volatility. VOLARE's realized variance series are second-moment statistics derived from ultra-high-frequency intraday returns at 5-minute sampling \citep{volare2026}; this quantity does not appear in any known training corpus.

\input{tables/table_pretraining_data}

Financial data constitutes less than 1\% of training observations for all TSFMs we evaluate, limited to daily exchange rates and macroeconomic indicators. Moreover, most public training corpora (e.g., Monash, LOTSA) use time series that end before or during the early portion of our out-of-sample evaluation period (2017--2026), reducing the scope for temporal overlap. This minimal financial exposure makes data contamination an unlikely explanation for TSFM performance on realized volatility. Instead, the results suggest that TSFM accuracy reflects transfer of general temporal patterns (mean reversion, long memory, regime persistence) rather than memorization of financial dynamics.

\subsection{Forecast Evaluation}
\label{subsec:evaluation}

\paragraph{Walk-forward design.}
We employ a walk-forward evaluation scheme with a rolling origin. Econometric models use a fixed estimation window of 252 trading days (approximately one year): at each origin, the model is estimated on the most recent 252 observations, and the window slides forward one day at a time, producing daily re-estimated forecasts. Foundation models follow the same walk-forward timing but use a 512-day context window and require no estimation step; only the context window slides forward. Because the TSFM context window is longer, the first TSFM forecast is produced 260 days later than the first econometric forecast; all model comparisons are conducted on the common out-of-sample period where both econometric and TSFM forecasts are available. For the equity sample (2,786 days), this produces approximately 2,148 common out-of-sample forecasts per model--asset combination at $h = 1$; the FX and futures samples (4,240+ days) yield approximately 3,602.

\paragraph{Forecast horizons.}
We evaluate three forecast horizons: $h = 1$ (one day), $h = 5$ (one week), and $h = 22$ (one month). The forecast target at horizon $h$ is the average realized volatility over the next $h$ days, $h^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^{h} RV_{t+i}$. All models produce direct $h$-step forecasts. For HAR-family models, this means estimating a separate regression for each horizon, where the dependent variable is the $h$-day average of future RV; the model does not iterate one-step-ahead predictions forward. ARFIMA similarly produces direct multi-step forecasts from its estimated ARMA representation on log RV. For TSFMs, each model generates a full forecast trajectory of length $h$ in a single inference call; the point forecast is the mean of this $h$-step output vector. This direct approach avoids the compounding of errors that arises in iterated multi-step forecasting.

\paragraph{Loss functions and statistical testing.}
% REVIEW NOTE (Comment 37): Merged "Statistical tests", "Forecast efficiency", and "Time-varying relative performance" into a single subsection.
Our primary loss function is the quasi-likelihood (QLIKE) loss of \citet{patton2011}:
\begin{equation}
  L_{\text{QLIKE}}(\hat{\sigma}_t^2, RV_t) = \frac{RV_t}{\hat{\sigma}_t^2} - \log\frac{RV_t}{\hat{\sigma}_t^2} - 1,
  \label{eq:qlike}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\sigma}_t^2$ is the forecast and $RV_t$ is the realized value. QLIKE preserves the ranking of forecasts regardless of the choice of volatility proxy, making it the preferred metric when the true conditional variance is unobserved. We also report mean squared error (MSE), mean absolute error (MAE), and the out-of-sample $R^2$ defined as $R^2_{\text{OOS}} = 1 - \sum_t (RV_t - \hat{f}_t)^2 / \sum_t (RV_t - \bar{RV})^2$, where $\bar{RV}$ is the historical mean of realized variance over the estimation window. Unlike the in-sample $R^2$, $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ can take negative values, indicating that the model's forecasts are less accurate than a simple historical mean forecast. All forecasts are floored at $10^{-10}$ to prevent numerical instability in the QLIKE calculation when models produce near-zero predictions.

Pairwise forecast comparisons use the DM test with Newey--West variance estimation, applied to QLIKE loss differentials. To address the multiple comparison problem inherent in evaluating fourteen models, we compute the MCS, which identifies the subset of models whose forecasting ability cannot be statistically distinguished from the best model at a given significance level. We use the $T_{\max}$ test statistic with a block bootstrap (block length $= 22$ days, $B = 10{,}000$ replications) and significance level $\alpha = 0.10$.

We assess forecast efficiency using the Mincer--Zarnowitz (MZ) regression \citep{mincer1969}:
\begin{equation}
  RV_t = \alpha + \beta \, \hat{f}_t + \varepsilon_t,
  \label{eq:mz}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{f}_t$ is the model's forecast. Under forecast optimality, $\alpha = 0$ and $\beta = 1$, meaning the forecast is unbiased and captures the correct scale of variation. We test the joint null $H_0\!: \alpha = 0, \beta = 1$ using a Wald test with Newey--West standard errors and report cross-asset averages and rejection rates at the 5\% level.

To examine whether relative forecast performance is stable over time, we apply the \citet{giacomini2010} fluctuation test (GR). For each model paired against the HAR benchmark, we compute a rolling DM statistic over a window of size $m = \lfloor 0.3 \times T \rfloor$, producing a time path of relative QLIKE performance. The test statistic is $\sup_t |S_t|$, compared against critical values from the distribution of the supremum of a standardized Brownian bridge. Rejection of the null indicates that the two models' relative performance changes significantly over time.

Moirai-MoE-S achieves the second-lowest QLIKE (0.233), followed by Moirai~2.0 (0.257). The econometric benchmarks now show near-zero or negative $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$, indicating that they barely outperform (or underperform) the historical mean at this horizon. Log-HAR achieves $R^2_{\text{OOS}} = -0.025$, while Sundial maintains a positive $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ of 0.148. Under median aggregation, the qualitative model ranking is preserved: Sundial achieves the best median QLIKE at $h = 1$ (0.181), followed by Moirai-MoE-S (0.195) and TimesFM~2.5 (0.203). Sundial and Moirai-MoE-S are tied for the lowest median QLIKE at $h = 5$ (both 0.149), while Sundial achieves the lowest median QLIKE at $h = 22$ (0.217). Across the equity sample, the $10^{-10}$ floor binds for fewer than 0.1\% of TSFM forecast--date pairs but for 2--5\% of HAR-RS and HARQ pairs, explaining their inflated QLIKE. Log-HAR and ARFIMA avoid this problem entirely by operating in log space, where forecasts are positive by construction. TSFMs also produce strictly positive forecasts in our evaluation, since their median output on a strictly positive input series does not cross zero. This practical advantage may be as important as any differences in statistical accuracy: a model that occasionally produces near-zero forecasts is difficult to deploy in production risk systems.

\paragraph{Foreign exchange.}
The FX sample covers five major currency pairs with lower volatility levels ($\bar{RV} \approx 3 \times 10^{-5}$) and moderate persistence ($\bar{\rho}_1 = 0.52$). For FX, Sundial achieves the lowest loss across all horizons and metrics. At $h = 1$, Sundial achieves the lowest QLIKE (0.153) and MSE ($0.162 \times 10^{-8}$), followed by Log-HAR (0.163) and TimesFM~2.5 (0.171). Moirai~2.0 underperforms substantially (QLIKE = 0.329, $R^2_{\text{OOS}} = -0.008$). \paragraph{Futures.}
The futures sample displays the widest cross-asset heterogeneity in volatility dynamics. The E-mini S\&P 500 (ES) has first-order autocorrelation of 0.779, while Gold (GC) shows near-zero autocorrelation ($\rho_1 = -0.001$). At $h = 1$, Sundial achieves the lowest QLIKE (0.189), followed by Log-HAR (0.193) and TimesFM~2.5 (0.204). Moirai~2.0 ranks behind (0.289). At $h = 5$, Sundial leads on QLIKE (0.139), followed by Moirai-MoE-S (0.155) and Log-HAR (0.165); Moirai-MoE-S achieves the highest $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ (0.316). At the monthly horizon, Sundial retains the QLIKE lead (0.184), followed by Moirai-MoE-S (0.206), Moirai~2.0 (0.238), and TimesFM~2.5 (0.275), while levels-based HAR variants produce inflated QLIKE (HAR: 1.08, HARQ: 17.88).

\paragraph{Cross-asset patterns.}
Three patterns emerge from the cross-asset comparison. First, Sundial achieves the lowest QLIKE across all three asset classes and all horizons (Tables~\ref{tab:main_results},~\ref{tab:fx_results},~\ref{tab:futures_results}), a level of consistency not achieved by any other model in the evaluation. Second, Log-HAR remains the best or near-best econometric specification across all asset classes, confirming the practical value of the log transformation for ensuring forecast positivity and aligning with QLIKE's loss structure. Third, within the TSFM class, performance dispersion is wide: Sundial, Moirai-MoE-S, TimesFM~2.5, and Moirai~2.0 form a competitive tier, while Toto underperforms across all asset classes and Lag-Llama underperforms at short horizons. Per-asset QLIKE breakdowns in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_tables} (Tables~\ref{tab:per_asset_qlike}--\ref{tab:per_asset_qlike_h22}) confirm that these rankings are not driven by outlier assets. These results highlight the importance of both multi-market and multi-model evaluations: a model that appears dominant on one asset class may not generalize.

\subsection{Drivers of TSFM Forecast Performance}
\label{subsec:drivers}

The cross-asset results above show that TSFM rankings vary across asset classes, raising the question of what asset-level characteristics drive relative forecast performance. \citet{carriero2024macro} find that TSFMs struggle with highly persistent macroeconomic series (first-order partial autocorrelation above 0.7). We test whether a similar pattern holds for realized volatility by examining the relationship between each asset's first-order autocorrelation $\rho_1$ and the TSFM's QLIKE ratio relative to HAR.

Figure~\ref{fig:persistence_drivers} plots QLIKE ratios (model / HAR) at $h = 1$ against $\rho_1$ for 50 assets, separately for each TSFM. Unlike the macroeconomic setting, we find no statistically significant relationship between persistence and TSFM relative performance: the rank correlation ranges from $r = 0.06$ ($p = 0.68$) for Chronos-Bolt to $r = -0.17$ ($p = 0.23$) for Lag-Llama, with the four new models showing similarly insignificant correlations. Sundial achieves lower QLIKE than HAR for 100\% of assets in both high- and low-persistence groups, confirming its consistent advantage regardless of persistence. Moirai-MoE-S beats HAR for 84\% of high-persistence and 68\% of low-persistence assets, while TimesFM~2.5 shows a modest persistence effect (76\% vs.\ 56\%).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_persistence_drivers.pdf}
  \caption{QLIKE ratio (model / HAR) at $h = 1$ vs.\ first-order autocorrelation $\rho_1$ for 50 assets. Values below one indicate the TSFM outperforms HAR. Each point is one asset, colored by class (blue: equities, red: FX, green: futures). Dashed line: OLS trend; $r$ and $p$ are the Pearson correlation and its $p$-value.}

This null result is informative (Figure~\ref{fig:persistence_drivers}). Realized volatility, unlike many macroeconomic series, is stationary and mean-reverting by construction. Even the least persistent assets in our sample (Gold, $\rho_1 \approx 0$) exhibit the positive, right-skewed distribution that TSFMs encounter frequently in their pretraining corpora. The variation in TSFM performance across assets is better explained by architecture than by persistence: Sundial achieves the lowest QLIKE across all asset classes regardless of individual $\rho_1$, while other TSFMs show asset-class-dependent strengths. This suggests that the relevant driver is the match between each TSFM's implicit prior (shaped by its training data composition and output mechanism) and the distributional characteristics of realized volatility, rather than the degree of serial dependence.

At $h = 5$, TSFMs continue to lead. Sundial again enters for 100\% of assets, followed by Moirai-MoE-S (71.8\%), Moirai~2.0 (25.6\%), and Log-HAR (17.9\%). Chronos-Bolt, TimesFM~2.5, Lag-Llama, and Toto are excluded from all MCS sets.

Lag-Llama's sharp improvement from 0\% at $h = 1$ to 67.5\% at $h = 22$ suggests that its lag-based tokenization captures long-range dependencies more effectively than short-term dynamics. The high ARFIMA inclusion rate at $h = 22$ is consistent with its long-memory parameterization: at the monthly horizon, fractional differencing captures slow mean reversion that the HAR's three fixed lags approximate less precisely.

\paragraph{DM pairwise tests.}
The DM test is computed pairwise: for each of the 14 models, we test it against each of the remaining 13 models on each of the 50 assets, yielding $14 \times 13 \times 50 = 9{,}100$ individual tests. Table~\ref{tab:dm_summary} reports the fraction of pairwise DM tests in which each model achieves significantly lower QLIKE at the 5\% level, aggregated across 50 assets and 13 opponents. At $h = 1$, Sundial wins the highest fraction of pairwise comparisons (87.8\%), followed by Moirai-MoE-S (60.3\%), Log-HAR (57.1\%), and TimesFM~2.5 (53.4\%). Sundial's win rate is 28 percentage points above the next-best model, indicating that its QLIKE advantage is both large and statistically significant across the vast majority of asset--model pairs. At $h = 5$ and $h = 22$, Sundial again achieves the highest win rate (78.8\% and 51.1\%), followed by Moirai-MoE-S (66.0\% and 37.1\%). Toto's win rate never exceeds 1.7\%, confirming its persistent miscalibration. Lag-Llama's win rate is 18.2\% at $h = 1$ and 18.5\% at $h = 22$; the modest DM win rate at $h = 22$, despite 67.5\% MCS inclusion, reflects the fact that forecast differences narrow at longer horizons, making it harder to reject equal predictive ability in pairwise tests. Figure~\ref{fig:cumulative_qlike} plots the cumulative QLIKE differential between Log-HAR and Moirai~2.0 at $h = 1$, showing that Moirai's advantage accumulates steadily after the COVID-19 onset.

\paragraph{Forecast efficiency: MZ regressions.}
Table~\ref{tab:mz} reports average MZ regression results across 50 assets. The MZ regression, $RV_t = \alpha + \beta \hat{f}_t + \varepsilon_t$, tests whether forecasts are efficient: under the null, $\alpha = 0$ and $\beta = 1$.

At $h = 1$, Moirai~2.0 and ARFIMA produce the most well-calibrated forecasts ($\hat{\beta} = 1.05$ for both), closest to the theoretical ideal of unity. Sundial ($\hat{\beta} = 1.14$) and TimesFM~2.5 ($\hat{\beta} = 1.18$) also remain close to unity. The HAR family overshoots modestly ($\hat{\beta} = 1.29$ for HAR, 1.25 for HAR-J), while Log-HAR overshoots substantially ($\hat{\beta} = 1.77$), reflecting the bias-correction step that converts log-space forecasts back to levels. Among TSFMs, TimesFM~2.5 achieves the highest MZ $R^2$ (0.432), followed by Chronos-Bolt-B (0.423) and Moirai-MoE-S (0.415). Two new models show systematic undershooting: Moirai-MoE-S ($\hat{\beta} = 0.79$) and Toto ($\hat{\beta} = 0.44$), indicating compressed forecasts. The joint efficiency null ($\alpha = 0$, $\beta = 1$) is rejected for 90--100\% of assets for most TSFMs (76\% for Lag-Llama), compared to 54--58\% for HAR and HAR-J. This seemingly paradoxical result (TSFMs are more accurate on MSE and $R^2$ but more frequently reject efficiency) arises because the MZ test has greater power when $R^2$ is high.

At $h = 22$ (Table~\ref{tab:mz_h22}), all econometric models exhibit substantial undershoot ($\hat{\beta} = 0.30$--$0.58$), with MZ $R^2$ falling to 0.07--0.14. TSFMs retain substantially better calibration: Moirai~2.0 achieves $\hat{\beta} = 0.99$, Sundial $\hat{\beta} = 0.89$, and TimesFM~2.5 the highest $R^2$ (0.235). Moirai-MoE-S undershoots more at this horizon ($\hat{\beta} = 0.48$), while Toto's severe undershoot ($\hat{\beta} = 0.26$) confirms its persistent scale misalignment. This confirms that the best TSFMs' advantage at longer horizons is not only in loss-function terms but also in the structural quality of their forecasts.

The GR tests reveal three patterns. \paragraph{Interpretation.}
Taken together, the four tests support the same conclusion: the best TSFMs produce statistically superior and better-calibrated forecasts than HAR-family models, but the TSFM class is not uniformly strong. The MCS identifies Sundial as the only model included for 97.5--100\% of equities at all horizons (Table~\ref{tab:mcs}), with Moirai-MoE-S as a consistent runner-up. The DM tests confirm that Sundial's win rate (87.8\% at $h = 1$; Table~\ref{tab:dm_summary}) far exceeds all other models. The MZ regressions show that the best TSFMs maintain calibration at long horizons ($\hat{\beta}$ closer to 1; Tables~\ref{tab:mz} and~\ref{tab:mz_h22}), while econometric models suffer severe undershoot at $h = 22$. The GR test reveals that relative performance is not constant (Figure~\ref{fig:gr_fluctuation}): Log-HAR's advantage over HAR erodes over time, and TSFMs consistently outperform HAR from their entry into the sample onward. The wide heterogeneity across TSFM architectures---from Sundial's consistent top ranking to Toto's miscalibration---suggests that pretraining data composition and output mechanism (flow matching vs.\ quantile regression) matter greatly.

A natural question is whether these rankings are stable across market regimes, estimation choices, and evaluation parameters. We assess the stability of our main findings along several dimensions.

\paragraph{Sub-sample stability: pre- and post-COVID.}
Table~\ref{tab:subsample} splits the equity sample at March 1, 2020, and reports forecast accuracy separately for the pre-COVID (2015--2020) and post-COVID (2020--2026) periods. The sub-sample analysis reveals that TSFM performance is stable across regimes, while levels-based econometric models are not.

In the pre-COVID period, Sundial achieves the lowest QLIKE at $h=1$ (0.198) and $h=5$ (0.179), while Log-HAR leads at $h=22$ (0.320). The pre-COVID period features lower average volatility and fewer extreme events, yet Sundial already outperforms all econometric models. Toto remains miscalibrated in both periods (QLIKE 34.1 pre-COVID, 34.8 post-COVID at $h=1$).

In the post-COVID period, the top TSFMs achieve the lowest loss across all metrics and horizons. Sundial achieves the lowest QLIKE at $h=1$ (0.193 vs.\ 0.224 for Log-HAR), $h=5$ (0.152 vs.\ 0.249), and $h=22$ (0.135 vs.\ 0.329). TimesFM~2.5 achieves the highest $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ at $h=1$ (0.445), while Sundial leads at $h=5$ (0.386) and $h=22$ (0.192). The levels-based HAR variants suffer extreme QLIKE inflation in the high-volatility regime: HARQ's QLIKE rises from 2.35 pre-COVID to 22.98 post-COVID at $h=1$, because the near-zero forecast problem is exacerbated when realized values are large. Log-HAR is more stable across regimes than its levels-based counterparts but still falls behind the TSFMs on all metrics in the post-COVID sample. The TSFM heterogeneity pattern persists across sub-samples: Lag-Llama underperforms in both periods at $h = 1$ (QLIKE 0.433 pre-COVID, 0.385 post-COVID) but improves at longer horizons. These results (Table~\ref{tab:subsample}) suggest that TSFMs' advantage is present in both calm and volatile market conditions, though the gap widens in the post-COVID regime where their flexible nonparametric forecasts adapt more effectively than the HAR's linear structure.

\paragraph{Estimation window: 252 vs.\ 512 days.}
A potential concern is that TSFMs receive 512 days of context while econometric models use 252-day estimation windows, creating an information asymmetry that may favor TSFMs. To address this, we re-estimate all six econometric benchmarks with a 512-day rolling window, matching the TSFM context length. Table~\ref{tab:window_512} reports the results at $h = 1$ for the 40-equity sample. Five of six models improve under the longer window: HAR $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ rises from 0.340 to 0.435, and levels-HAR QLIKE inflation decreases substantially (e.g., HAR-RS QLIKE drops from 3.48 to 0.57). The exception is ARFIMA, whose long-memory estimation degrades with a 512-day window ($R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ falls from 0.132 to $-0.332$). Cross-model $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ rankings are highly stable (Spearman $\rho = 0.94$, $p < 0.01$). Even after granting econometric models the same 512-day information set, the best TSFM zero-shot QLIKE values (Sundial at 0.195, Moirai-MoE-S at 0.211; Table~\ref{tab:main_results}) remain competitive with or below the best 512-day econometric QLIKE (Log-HAR at 0.20), confirming that the TSFM advantage is not an artifact of the longer context window. The same pattern holds at longer horizons: Tables~\ref{tab:window_512_h5} and~\ref{tab:window_512_h22} in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_tables} confirm that the 512-day window improves all HAR variants at $h = 5$ and $h = 22$, while ARFIMA deteriorates further.

\paragraph{Context window length.}
Table~\ref{tab:context_sensitivity} examines how TSFM forecast accuracy varies with context window length (128, 256, and 512 days) for six models (TimesFM~2.5 does not support shorter context lengths; Toto is omitted due to severe miscalibration at all context lengths). Longer context generally improves Chronos-Bolt: QLIKE decreases from 0.231 (ctx=128) to 0.222 (ctx=512) at $h = 1$, with similar patterns at $h = 5$. Moirai~2.0 shows a more nuanced pattern: while longer context improves QLIKE at $h = 1$ (0.264 to 0.243), it \emph{worsens} performance at $h = 22$ (0.239 at ctx=128 vs.\ 0.251 at ctx=512), suggesting that Moirai's architecture captures long-range dependencies efficiently even from shorter windows and that additional context introduces noise at the monthly horizon. Lag-Llama benefits substantially from longer context at $h = 5$ and $h = 22$ (QLIKE drops from 0.515 to 0.297 at $h = 5$), but at $h = 1$ the relationship is non-monotonic, with the best QLIKE at ctx=256 (0.380).

Among the newer models, Sundial shows the strongest context dependence: QLIKE improves monotonically with context length at all horizons, from 0.206 (ctx=128) to 0.190 (ctx=512) at $h = 1$ and from 0.239 to 0.204 at $h = 22$. Sundial's $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ at ctx=128 is negative at all horizons, indicating that 128 days of context is insufficient for its flow-matching architecture to produce useful forecasts. Moirai-MoE-S shows moderate context sensitivity on QLIKE, with the best performance at ctx=256 for $h = 5$ and $h = 22$ (0.164 and 0.212, respectively), though the differences from ctx=512 are small. These results confirm that the 512-day default is appropriate for most models but not universally optimal: Moirai~2.0 and Moirai-MoE-S prefer shorter context at the monthly horizon, while Sundial requires the full 512-day window.

\paragraph{QLIKE floor sensitivity.}
Our QLIKE implementation floors forecasts at $10^{-10}$ to prevent division by zero. Because levels-based HAR variants occasionally produce near-zero forecasts, this floor choice could affect model rankings. Table~\ref{tab:qlike_floor_sensitivity} reports cross-asset mean QLIKE at $h = 1$ under four floor values ($10^{-4}$, $10^{-6}$, $10^{-8}$, $10^{-10}$). QLIKE values are identical for floors $\leq 10^{-6}$ (Spearman rank correlation $\rho = 1.0$ vs.\ $10^{-10}$), confirming that the floor never binds at these levels. Only the aggressive $10^{-4}$ floor alters rankings ($\rho = 0.82$) by clipping levels-HAR forecasts that drive QLIKE inflation (e.g., HARQ drops from 16.04 to 0.38). Our default $10^{-10}$ floor is conservative and does not distort model comparisons.

\paragraph{MZ bias-corrected evaluation.}
A model's low loss could reflect good calibration (i.e., alignment between the scale of forecasts and realizations) rather than genuine predictive information about future volatility dynamics. The MZ regression provides a natural correction: by regressing realized values on forecasts, one obtains intercept and slope parameters that remove any linear bias. If a model's accuracy survives this correction, its forecasts contain information beyond what a simple affine rescaling could produce. To test this, we apply a recursive MZ bias correction to all TSFM forecasts at $h = 1$: at each time $t$, we estimate $\hat{\alpha}_t$ and $\hat{\beta}_t$ from all observations up to $t - 1$, then form the corrected forecast $\hat{f}_t^{\text{MZ}} = \hat{\alpha}_t + \hat{\beta}_t f_t$ using an expanding estimation window (minimum 252 days). Table~\ref{tab:mz_bias_corrected} compares original and MZ-corrected TSFM forecasts against econometric benchmarks, evaluated on the common post-warm-up sample. MZ correction improves QLIKE for most well-calibrated models (TimesFM~2.5: 0.2221 $\to$ 0.1887; Chronos-Bolt-Base: 0.2307 $\to$ 0.1918), while Sundial's already-optimal QLIKE is essentially unchanged (0.1950 $\to$ 0.1945). MZ correction dramatically improves Toto (35.69 $\to$ 3.94), indicating that much of Toto's poor performance stems from severe scale misalignment rather than absence of signal. Lag-Llama worsens under correction, where the linear adjustment pushes some forecasts toward zero. Moirai-MoE-S improves modestly (0.2098 $\to$ 0.2038), while Moirai~2.0 is nearly unchanged (0.2245 $\to$ 0.2238), indicating that both Moirai variants are already well calibrated at this horizon. MSE and $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ are largely unchanged for the well-calibrated models, confirming that TSFM accuracy reflects genuine predictive content rather than calibration artifacts.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper evaluates eight zero-shot time series foundation models for realized volatility forecasting across 50 assets spanning U.S.\ equities, foreign exchange, and futures.

Three main findings emerge. First, the best zero-shot TSFMs outperform the best econometric benchmarks for univariate realized variance forecasting (Tables~\ref{tab:main_results},~\ref{tab:fx_results}, and~\ref{tab:futures_results}), contrasting with prior negative findings on daily returns and on TimesFM~1.0. Sundial, a flow-matching generative model, achieves the lowest QLIKE across all three asset classes and all horizons, entering the Model Confidence Set for 97.5--100\% of equities (Table~\ref{tab:mcs}). However, performance is highly heterogeneous across TSFM architectures: Toto produces severely miscalibrated forecasts (QLIKE exceeding 6 at every horizon; Table~\ref{tab:main_results}), though MZ bias correction reveals underlying signal (Table~\ref{tab:mz_bias_corrected}), while Sundial, Moirai-MoE-S, and TimesFM~2.5 match or exceed the best econometric benchmarks. Second, the advantage of the top-performing TSFMs grows with forecast horizon: at $h = 1$, Sundial's QLIKE (0.195) leads Log-HAR (0.219) by 11\%, but at $h = 22$ the gap widens to 37\% (0.218 vs.\ 0.345). This pattern holds for Moirai-MoE-S as well, though not for all TSFMs. Third, rankings within the TSFM class are more variable than across classes: Sundial achieves the lowest QLIKE for equities, FX, and futures alike, while the relative ordering of other TSFMs (Moirai-MoE-S, Moirai~2.0, TimesFM~2.5, Chronos-Bolt) shifts across asset classes.

Several limitations should be noted. We evaluate only zero-shot TSFM performance; parameter-efficient fine-tuning may yield further gains, though the adaptation mechanisms differ across architectures (Chronos-2 supports LoRA-based adaptation through its encoder-decoder structure, while Lag-Llama uses epoch-based training through its GluonTS estimator interface). Our evaluation is restricted to point forecasts; evaluating the full predictive distributions that several TSFMs produce, using proper scoring rules such as the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS), remains an open question. VOLARE provides alternative realized measures (subsampled realized variance, realized kernels, range-based estimators) whose effect on model rankings we leave for future work. Extension to realized covariance forecasting and portfolio construction is a natural next step. While no TSFM in our study was trained on realized volatility (Section~\ref{subsec:contamination}), indirect exposure to related financial series such as exchange rates and macroeconomic indicators cannot be fully ruled out as a contributing factor; future work with held-out asset classes or temporal holdouts could further address this concern.

For practitioners, three concrete recommendations emerge. First, Sundial is the recommended default for zero-shot realized volatility forecasting: it achieves the lowest QLIKE across all asset classes and horizons (Tables~\ref{tab:main_results},~\ref{tab:fx_results},~\ref{tab:futures_results}), enters the MCS for 97.5--100\% of equities (Table~\ref{tab:mcs}), and its already near-optimal calibration means that post-hoc MZ bias correction yields negligible further improvement (Table~\ref{tab:mz_bias_corrected}). Second, for monthly-horizon forecasting ($h = 22$), TSFMs offer a substantial improvement over HAR-family models, since the best econometric models barely outperform the historical mean ($R^2_{\text{OOS}} = -0.025$ for Log-HAR; Table~\ref{tab:main_results}) while Sundial maintains $R^2_{\text{OOS}} = 0.148$. Third, MZ bias correction can rescue underperforming TSFMs---Toto's QLIKE drops from 35.69 to 3.94 after rescaling (Table~\ref{tab:mz_bias_corrected}), revealing underlying signal beneath its severe miscalibration---suggesting that a simple post-processing step may extend the usable TSFM set.

More broadly, the finding that models pretrained on weather, energy, and retail data can forecast financial volatility without any domain-specific training raises questions about the nature of temporal patterns that transfer across domains. Realized volatility shares key statistical properties---positivity, mean reversion, long memory, right-skewed distributions---with many physical and economic processes, and TSFMs appear to exploit these shared structures. At the same time, the wide performance dispersion across architectures cautions against treating TSFMs as a uniform class. Model selection within the TSFM family is as important as the choice between TSFMs and econometric models.
