\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Accurate forecasting of realized volatility is central to risk management, derivative pricing, and portfolio allocation.
To date, no study has systematically compared multiple zero-shot time series foundation models against standard econometric benchmarks for realized volatility using formal statistical testing.
We evaluate four time series foundation models (TSFMs)---Chronos-Bolt, Moirai~2.0, Lag-Llama, and Kronos---against six econometric specifications (HAR, HAR-J, HAR-RS, HARQ, Log-HAR, ARFIMA) on 50 assets from the VOLARE dataset (equities, FX, futures; 2015--2026) at horizons of 1, 5, and 22 days, using Diebold--Mariano tests and Model Confidence Sets.
The best TSFMs match or exceed the best econometric benchmarks, but rankings depend on loss function, horizon, and architecture. At $h = 1$, Log-HAR leads on QLIKE while Moirai~2.0 dominates MSE and $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$; at $h \geq 5$, Moirai~2.0 leads on all metrics. TSFM performance is heterogeneous: Kronos is excluded from the MCS for virtually all assets, while Lag-Llama enters for 95\% at $h = 22$ but 0\% at $h = 1$. Rankings shift across asset classes, with Chronos-Bolt outperforming on FX and futures. For covariance forecasting, element-wise TSFMs achieve competitive accuracy but fail to produce viable portfolios at high dimensions, where only structurally constrained approaches succeed.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Realized volatility, foundation models, time series forecasting, HAR, ARFIMA, VOLARE

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} C22, C53, C55, G17
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================

Volatility is a first-order input to option pricing, value-at-risk estimation, and portfolio optimization. Since the work of \citet{andersen1998} and \citet{barndorffnielsen2002}, realized volatility constructed from high-frequency returns has served as the standard model-free measure of ex-post price variation. Forecasting realized volatility is now a central problem in financial econometrics, with direct implications for risk management, hedging, and capital allocation.

The Heterogeneous Autoregressive (HAR) model of \citet{corsi2009} established itself as the dominant benchmark. Its three-component structure---aggregating past realized volatility at daily, weekly, and monthly frequencies---provides a parsimonious approximation to the long-memory dynamics that characterize volatility. Subsequent extensions have incorporated jump variation \citep{andersen2007roughing}, semivariance asymmetries \citep{patton2015}, and measurement error corrections \citep{bollerslev2016harq}, yet the practical gains over the baseline specification have been mixed \citep{clements2021practical, branco2024}.

Machine learning methods---including random forests \citep{luong2018}, neural networks \citep{bucci2020}, and convolutional architectures \citep{morenopino2024deepvol}---have also been applied to realized volatility forecasting. Results are inconsistent: \citet{bucci2020} reports gains over HAR, while \citet{branco2024} finds that linear models are difficult to outperform once the comparison accounts for multiple testing. A broader assessment by \citet{christensen2023ml} concludes that machine learning offers incremental improvements but no paradigm shift for volatility prediction.

A different class of models has emerged over the past two years: time series foundation models (TSFMs), large pretrained transformers trained on billions of time series observations across domains. Leading examples include Chronos \citep{ansari2024chronos}, TimesFM \citep{das2024timesfm}, and Moirai \citep{woo2024moirai}, each of which has since released second-generation versions with improved architectures and expanded training data \citep{ansari2025chronos2, liu2025moirai2}. These models can produce forecasts for previously unseen time series without any retraining---a capability known as zero-shot forecasting. Several surveys document the rapid pace of development in this area \citep{liang2024tsfmsurvey, ye2024tsfmsurvey, miller2024tsfmsurvey}.

Applications of TSFMs to financial time series remain limited. \citet{goel2025rv} tested TimesFM on realized volatility for 20 stocks and found that fine-tuning was necessary for the model to compete with HAR; zero-shot performance was poor. \citet{rahimikia2025revisiting} evaluated Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, and MOMENT on daily stock returns and reported uniformly negative zero-shot results, with simple ARIMA models dominating. However, realized volatility differs from returns in ways that may favor foundation models: it is strictly positive, exhibits mean reversion, displays long-memory dependence, and follows a distribution much closer to the macroeconomic and physical series on which TSFMs were pretrained. To our knowledge, no study has yet evaluated multiple foundation models on realized volatility with formal statistical testing.

This paper makes five contributions. First, we provide the first multi-model TSFM comparison for realized volatility forecasting, evaluating four TSFMs (Chronos-Bolt, Moirai~2.0, Lag-Llama, and Kronos) against six econometric specifications across three asset classes. Second, we test these models in a pure zero-shot setting---no domain-specific training---to isolate the value of general time series pretraining. Third, we analyze three forecast horizons ($h = 1, 5, 22$ days) across 50 assets, uncovering horizon-dependent and asset-class-dependent patterns in relative model performance. Fourth, we apply Model Confidence Set analysis \citep{hansen2011mcs} to control for multiple comparisons, moving beyond pairwise tests. Fifth, we use the recently released VOLARE dataset \citep{volare2026}, which provides methodologically consistent realized measures across U.S.\ equities, FX, and futures, ensuring that cross-asset comparisons are not confounded by measurement differences.

Our main findings are as follows. First, zero-shot TSFMs are competitive with the best econometric benchmarks for realized volatility forecasting---a result that stands in contrast to prior negative findings on daily returns \citep{rahimikia2025revisiting} and the zero-shot evaluation in \citet{goel2025rv}. Second, the ranking of models depends on the loss function: Log-HAR achieves the lowest QLIKE at the one-day horizon for equities, but TSFMs dominate on MSE, MAE, and out-of-sample $R^2$ at all horizons. Moirai~2.0 achieves the lowest QLIKE at the 5-day and 22-day horizons. Third, Model Confidence Set analysis shows that Moirai~2.0 enters the 90\% MCS for every equity at $h = 5$ and for 97.5\% of equities at $h = 22$, while Log-HAR enters for 90\% at $h = 1$. Fourth, model rankings are not stable across asset classes: Chronos-Bolt performs best for FX and futures, where volatility dynamics are less persistent, while Moirai~2.0 dominates for equities. Fifth, levels-based HAR extensions (HAR-RS, HARQ) produce near-zero forecasts that inflate QLIKE, a practical limitation that log-space and TSFM approaches avoid.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:literature} reviews the related literature. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data and sample construction. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} presents the econometric and foundation model specifications, along with the forecast evaluation framework. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the empirical results. Section~\ref{sec:robustness} contains robustness checks, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Realized Volatility Modeling}
\label{subsec:rv_modeling}

The theory of realized volatility originates with \citet{andersen1998} and \citet{andersen2001exchange}, who showed that the sum of squared intraday returns provides a consistent, nonparametric estimator of the integrated variance of asset prices. \citet{barndorffnielsen2002} formalized this result in a semimartingale framework, establishing that realized variance converges to integrated variance as the sampling frequency increases. Subsequent work identified the key stylized facts of realized volatility: approximate log-normality, long-memory dependence with a fractional integration parameter $d \approx 0.4$, and slow mean reversion \citep{andersen2003}.

At ultra-high sampling frequencies, microstructure noise from bid-ask bounce and discrete price changes biases the realized variance estimator upward. \citet{barndorffnielsen2008} introduced the realized kernel as a noise-consistent alternative that remains valid in the presence of market microstructure effects.

The HAR model of \citet{corsi2009} became the standard forecasting benchmark for realized volatility. By including daily, weekly, and monthly RV components as regressors, the model approximates long-memory behavior through a cascade of time scales corresponding to heterogeneous trading horizons. The specification is parsimonious---three regressors plus a constant---yet achieves accuracy comparable to far more complex alternatives.

A large body of work has extended the HAR framework. \citet{andersen2007roughing} and \citet{barndorffnielsen2004bipower} separated realized variance into continuous and jump components using bipower variation, yielding the HAR-J model. \citet{patton2015} and \citet{barndorffnielsen2010semivariance} decomposed realized variance into positive and negative semivariance to capture asymmetric responses to upside and downside moves. \citet{bollerslev2016harq} introduced the HARQ model, which interacts the daily RV regressor with realized quarticity to account for time-varying measurement error. \citet{clements2021practical} provides a practical guide to implementing and comparing these extensions.

Long-memory models offer a complementary approach. ARFIMA models \citep{baillie1996figarch, izzeldin2019} explicitly parameterize the fractional integration order, while the rough volatility framework of \citet{gatheral2018rough} models volatility as a fractional Brownian motion with Hurst exponent $H \approx 0.1$. \citet{bennedsen2022} provides cross-asset evidence on the roughness of realized volatility, though the debate over whether roughness is genuine or an artifact of microstructure noise continues.

Machine learning methods have been applied to realized volatility forecasting with mixed results. \citet{bucci2020} reports that feedforward and recurrent neural networks outperform HAR on a sample of U.S.\ stocks. \citet{luong2018} finds gains from random forests. \citet{morenopino2024deepvol} introduces DeepVol, a convolutional neural network that processes raw order book data. In contrast, \citet{branco2024} concludes that simple linear models are difficult to beat after correcting for multiple testing, and \citet{christensen2023ml} finds that machine learning offers only incremental improvements over standard econometric methods. Recent surveys by \citet{gunnarsson2024} and \citet{leushuis2026} cover this literature.

\subsection{Time Series Foundation Models}
\label{subsec:tsfm}

Time series foundation models are large pretrained models that produce forecasts for arbitrary time series without task-specific training, analogous to large language models for text \citep{liang2024tsfmsurvey}. These models are trained on vast corpora of time series drawn from diverse domains---weather, energy, retail, transport, macroeconomics---and learn general temporal patterns that transfer to unseen series at inference time.

Three architectural families dominate the first generation of TSFMs. Chronos \citep{ansari2024chronos} converts continuous values into discrete tokens via quantile binning and uses a T5 encoder-decoder architecture for autoregressive forecasting. TimesFM \citep{das2024timesfm} employs a patch-based decoder-only transformer with 200 million parameters, processing input in fixed-length patches and generating forecasts autoregressively. Moirai \citep{woo2024moirai} handles arbitrary numbers of variates through an ``any-variate'' attention mechanism and uses mixture distribution outputs for uncertainty quantification.

Second-generation models appeared in 2025 with marked improvements. \citet{ansari2025chronos2} introduced Chronos-2 and Chronos-Bolt: the former adds multivariate support and covariate handling, while the latter is a distilled encoder-only variant that produces direct quantile forecasts and runs up to 250 times faster than the original. Moirai~2.0 \citep{liu2025moirai2} demonstrated that smaller, better-trained models can match or exceed their larger predecessors---a ``less is more'' finding---using multi-token prediction and improved tokenization. Other models include Lag-Llama \citep{rasul2024lagllama}, MOMENT \citep{goswami2024moment}, Timer \citep{liu2024timer}, and Sundial \citep{liu2025sundial}, each exploring different design choices for temporal representation and output distribution.

Standardized benchmarks have been developed to evaluate TSFMs across domains. GIFT-Eval \citep{aksu2024gifteval} provides a unified evaluation protocol across multiple datasets and forecast horizons. FEV-Bench \citep{shchur2025fevbench} focuses on forecasting evaluation with emphasis on calibration. TSFM-Bench \citep{li2024tsfmbench} compares models across zero-shot and fine-tuned settings. A consistent finding is that no single model dominates across all domains and horizons, which motivates domain-specific evaluations such as the one we undertake here. \citet{adler2025calibration} raises additional concerns about the calibration of TSFM prediction intervals, finding systematic miscoverage across several models.

\subsection{Foundation Models for Volatility Forecasting}
\label{subsec:fm_volatility}

The application of TSFMs to volatility forecasting is recent and limited. The closest prior work is \citet{goel2025rv}, who tested TimesFM~1.0 on realized volatility for 20 stocks. They found that zero-shot TimesFM underperformed HAR and that fine-tuning was necessary to achieve competitive accuracy. Our paper differs in several respects: we evaluate four foundation models from different families (Chronos-Bolt, Moirai~2.0, Lag-Llama, and Kronos), use newer model generations, and apply Model Confidence Set analysis to control for multiple comparisons. The same research group also applied TimesFM to Value-at-Risk forecasting \citep{goel2024var}.

\citet{rahimikia2025revisiting} evaluated Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, and MOMENT on daily stock returns and found that zero-shot TSFMs consistently underperformed ARIMA. However, daily returns differ from realized volatility in fundamental ways: returns are approximately symmetric, near-white-noise, and lack the strong serial dependence that characterizes realized volatility. Realized volatility is strictly positive, mean-reverting, and displays long-memory dynamics---properties that bring it closer to the macroeconomic and physical time series on which TSFMs were pretrained. This distinction suggests that negative results on returns need not carry over to realized measures.

Other financial applications include stock price forecasting \citep{laniewski2025chronos, valeyre2024chronosstocks}, foreign exchange volatility modeling \citep{nguyen2025volabert}, and multivariate financial time series \citep{marconi2025}. Finance-specific foundation models have also been proposed, including FinCast \citep{zhu2025fincast} and the finance-oriented Kronos \citep{shi2025kronos}, though neither is publicly available at the time of writing. We include the general-purpose Kronos-T5 model \citep{kronos2024} (distinct from the finance-specific variant) in our evaluation alongside three other publicly available TSFMs.

The question of whether to fine-tune or apply TSFMs zero-shot is itself an active area of research. \citet{das2025icf} explored in-context fine-tuning for TimesFM, while \citet{germanmorales2024lora} and \citet{gupta2024beyondlora} investigated parameter-efficient adaptation via Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). Testing zero-shot performance first establishes an important baseline: if a TSFM already performs well without adaptation, the marginal value of fine-tuning may be limited, and the zero-shot workflow offers practical advantages in simplicity and computational cost.

We contribute to this emerging literature by providing the first evaluation that compares multiple time series foundation models against a broad set of econometric benchmarks for realized volatility, with formal statistical testing via Diebold--Mariano tests and Model Confidence Sets.

\section{Data and Sample Construction}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{The VOLARE Dataset}
\label{subsec:volare}

We use the VOLARE (VOLatility Archive for Realized Estimates) dataset of \citet{volare2026}, which provides daily realized volatility measures for a broad cross-section of financial assets. VOLARE is constructed from ultra-high-frequency tick data sourced from Kibot, covering 40 U.S.\ equities, 5 major currency pairs, and 5 commodity and index futures contracts. The equity sample begins on January 2, 2015 and runs through January 30, 2026, yielding 2,786 trading days per stock. The FX and futures samples begin on September 25, 2009 (4,242 and 4,228 trading days, respectively).

For each asset-day, VOLARE supplies a suite of realized measures computed at multiple sampling frequencies. We use the 5-minute realized variance ($RV^{(5)}$) as our primary target, along with 5-minute bipower variation ($BPV^{(5)}$), positive and negative realized semivariance ($RS^{+(5)}$, $RS^{-(5)}$), and 5-minute realized quarticity ($RQ^{(5)}$). All measures are expressed in decimal squared returns---for instance, a typical daily $RV^{(5)}$ for a U.S.\ equity is approximately $1.9 \times 10^{-4}$, corresponding to an annualized volatility of roughly 22\%.

Three features distinguish VOLARE from earlier datasets such as the Oxford-Man realized library. First, the realized measures are constructed using a uniform methodology across all assets, eliminating cross-asset inconsistencies that arise when measures are drawn from heterogeneous sources. Second, the dataset includes subsampled estimators ($RV^{(5)}_{\text{SS}}$), realized kernels, and range-based volatility estimators alongside the standard measures, enabling robustness checks across estimator types. Third, the multi-asset-class coverage---equities, currencies, and futures---permits tests of whether model rankings are stable across markets with different microstructure characteristics and volatility dynamics.

\subsection{Sample Selection and Descriptive Statistics}
\label{subsec:descriptive}

Our equity sample comprises the 40 stocks in VOLARE that span the full 2015--2026 period: AAPL, ADBE, AMD, AMGN, AMZN, AXP, BA, CAT, CRM, CSCO, CVX, DIS, GE, GOOGL, GS, HD, HON, IBM, JNJ, JPM, KO, MCD, META, MMM, MRK, MSFT, NFLX, NKE, NVDA, ORCL, PG, PM, SHW, TRV, TSLA, UNH, V, VZ, WMT, and XOM. These cover all 11 Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) sectors and include both mega-cap (AAPL, MSFT) and mid-cap (SHW, PM) names. The FX sample consists of five major currency pairs (AUDUSD, EURUSD, GBPUSD, USDCAD, USDJPY) and the futures sample covers five contracts (Corn, Crude Oil, E-mini S\&P 500, Gold, Natural Gas).

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Econometric Benchmarks}
\label{subsec:benchmarks}

We consider six econometric specifications that span the main approaches to realized volatility forecasting: the HAR family and its extensions, which exploit multi-scale aggregation, and ARFIMA, which explicitly models long memory.

\paragraph{HAR Model.}
The Heterogeneous Autoregressive model of \citet{corsi2009} captures the multi-scale persistence of realized volatility by aggregating past RV at daily, weekly, and monthly frequencies:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 RV_t + \beta_2 RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 RV_{t-22:t} + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:har}
\end{equation}
where $RV_{t-k:t} \equiv k^{-1}\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} RV_{t-i}$ denotes the average realized volatility over the previous $k$ days. This three-component structure approximates long-memory behavior through the actions of heterogeneous market participants---short-term traders, medium-term portfolio managers, and long-term institutional investors---who operate at different time horizons \citep{corsi2009}.

\paragraph{HAR-J Model.}
The HAR-J model augments the baseline HAR with a jump component to separate continuous and discontinuous variation \citep{andersen2007roughing}:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 RV_t + \beta_2 RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 RV_{t-22:t} + \beta_J J_t + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:har_j}
\end{equation}
where $J_t = \max(RV_t - BPV_t,\, 0)$ measures the jump component as the positive part of the difference between realized variance and bipower variation \citep{barndorffnielsen2004bipower}. A negative coefficient on $J_t$ indicates that large jumps reduce, rather than increase, future volatility.

\paragraph{HAR-RS Model.}
The HAR-RS model decomposes realized variance into positive and negative semivariance components to capture asymmetric volatility responses \citep{patton2015}:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1^+ RS_t^+ + \beta_1^- RS_t^- + \beta_2^+ RS_{t-5:t}^+ + \beta_2^- RS_{t-5:t}^- + \beta_3^+ RS_{t-22:t}^+ + \beta_3^- RS_{t-22:t}^- + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:har_rs}
\end{equation}
where $RS_t^+$ and $RS_t^-$ denote good and bad realized semivariance, respectively, and $RS_t^+ + RS_t^- = RV_t$ by construction \citep{barndorffnielsen2010semivariance}. The six-regressor structure allows upside and downside risk to follow separate dynamics at each aggregation frequency.

\paragraph{HARQ Model.}
The HARQ model of \citet{bollerslev2016harq} accounts for time-varying measurement error in realized volatility by interacting the daily RV regressor with the square root of realized quarticity:
\begin{equation}
  RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + (\beta_1 + \beta_1^Q \sqrt{RQ_t})\, RV_t + \beta_2 RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 RV_{t-22:t} + \varepsilon_{t+h},
  \label{eq:harq}
\end{equation}
where $RQ_t$ is the realized quarticity. The interaction term attenuates the daily RV signal when measurement noise is high, as indicated by large values of $RQ_t$.

\paragraph{Log-HAR Model.}
The Log-HAR model applies the HAR specification to log-transformed realized volatility \citep{andersen2003}:
\begin{equation}
  \log RV_{t+h} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \log RV_t + \beta_2 \log RV_{t-5:t} + \beta_3 \log RV_{t-22:t} + \varepsilon_{t+h}.
  \label{eq:log_har}
\end{equation}
The logarithmic transformation brings the RV distribution closer to normality, improving OLS efficiency. Point forecasts in levels are recovered via bias-corrected retransformation: $\widehat{RV}_{t+h} = \exp(\hat{y}_{t+h} + \hat{\sigma}^2/2)$, where $\hat{\sigma}^2$ is the estimated residual variance.

\paragraph{ARFIMA Model.}
The ARFIMA model captures the well-documented long-memory property of realized volatility through fractional differencing \citep{baillie1996figarch}:
\begin{equation}
  (1 - L)^d\, \Phi(L)\,(\log RV_t - \mu) = \Theta(L)\,\varepsilon_t,
  \label{eq:arfima}
\end{equation}
where $d \in (0, 0.5)$ is the fractional integration parameter, $\Phi(L)$ and $\Theta(L)$ are autoregressive and moving average lag polynomials of orders $p$ and $q$ (with $p, q \leq 2$ selected by AIC), and $L$ is the lag operator. The parameter $d$ is estimated by maximum likelihood. The fractional differencing filter is truncated at $K = 100$ lags.

All HAR-family models are estimated by OLS with Newey--West heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent standard errors. The ARFIMA model is re-estimated every 22 trading days to balance computational cost against parameter staleness.

We do not include the Realized GARCH model of \citet{hansen2012} as a benchmark. Realized GARCH requires daily returns as an additional input, which VOLARE does not provide, and no publicly available Python implementation exists---the standard \texttt{arch} package supports only GARCH and EGARCH variants. Implementation via \texttt{rpy2} and the R \texttt{rugarch} package is feasible but introduces cross-language dependencies that complicate reproducibility. We note that the HAR-J and HARQ specifications already incorporate jump variation and measurement error effects that Realized GARCH targets through a different mechanism.

\subsection{Time Series Foundation Models}
\label{subsec:tsfm_method}

We evaluate four time series foundation models (TSFMs) in a zero-shot setting---applied directly to realized volatility series without any domain-specific training or fine-tuning. Among the four TSFMs we evaluate, Chronos-Bolt and Moirai~2.0 are inference-only architectures with no public fine-tuning API; for these models, zero-shot is the only deployment mode. Lag-Llama supports fine-tuning through its GluonTS estimator interface (setting training epochs $> 0$), and the full Chronos-2 model (distinct from the distilled Bolt variant) provides built-in LoRA fine-tuning via its \texttt{fit()} method \citep{ansari2025chronos2}. We evaluate fine-tuned Lag-Llama in Section~\ref{sec:robustness}; Chronos-2 LoRA fine-tuning requires GPU resources and is left for future work.\footnote{We originally planned to include TimesFM 2.5 \citep{das2024timesfm}, but its publicly released model weights are incompatible with the current software package, preventing reliable inference.}

\paragraph{General pipeline.}
Because TSFMs originate in the machine learning literature, we briefly describe the forecasting pipeline in terms familiar to econometricians. Every TSFM follows three steps: \emph{tokenization}, \emph{encoding}, and \emph{decoding}. First, the raw time series---here, a vector of 512 daily RV observations---is converted into a sequence of discrete or continuous \emph{tokens}, the model's internal representation of the data. Tokenization serves the same role as variable construction in an econometric model: it determines which features of the history the model can ``see.'' Different TSFMs tokenize differently (quantile binning, lag features, kernel embeddings), and we describe each below. Second, the token sequence is processed by a \emph{transformer}, a neural network architecture in which each position in the sequence attends to all other positions via learned attention weights. The attention mechanism allows the model to capture dependencies at arbitrary lags without imposing the specific functional form that HAR prescribes (daily, weekly, monthly components). The transformer's parameters---attention weights, layer norms, feed-forward coefficients---are fixed during our evaluation; they were learned during pretraining on large external corpora (tens of billions of time series observations drawn from weather, energy, retail, transport, and macroeconomic domains). Third, the transformer's output is passed through a \emph{decoding head} that maps the internal representation back to forecasts in the original scale. Decoding heads differ across models: some output quantiles of the predictive distribution directly, others parameterize an explicit probability distribution (e.g., Student-$t$) from which forecast trajectories are sampled. In both cases, we extract the median as the point forecast, analogous to reporting the conditional median from a quantile regression.

\paragraph{Chronos-Bolt.}
Chronos-Bolt is a distilled variant of the Chronos family \citep{ansari2024chronos, ansari2025chronos2}. The original Chronos model tokenizes continuous values by mapping them into 4,096 discrete bins via quantile binning---each observation is replaced by the index of its nearest quantile in a reference distribution, much as one would discretize a continuous variable into histogram bins. The model then processes these token indices through a T5 encoder-decoder transformer and generates forecasts autoregressively, predicting one token at a time. Chronos-Bolt replaces this two-step architecture with an encoder-only transformer that reads the entire input context and produces all forecast quantiles in a single forward pass, without iterative decoding. This yields a 250$\times$ speedup over the original while maintaining competitive accuracy \citep{ansari2025chronos2}. The decoding head outputs quantile forecasts directly (e.g., the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles), bypassing the need to sample from a token distribution. We use both the small (48M parameters) and base (205M parameters) checkpoints (\texttt{amazon/chronos-bolt-small}, \texttt{amazon/chronos-bolt-base}) and take the median (0.5 quantile) as the point forecast.

\paragraph{Moirai 2.0.}
Moirai 2.0 \citep{liu2025moirai2} builds on the original Moirai architecture \citep{woo2024moirai}. It tokenizes the input series by dividing it into fixed-length \emph{patches}---contiguous blocks of consecutive observations (e.g., 32 days each)---and projects each patch into the transformer's embedding space via a learned linear map. This is analogous to constructing non-overlapping moving-window summaries of the series, except that the summary is learned rather than hand-specified. The transformer is decoder-only: it processes patches causally (each patch attends only to itself and earlier patches), preserving the temporal ordering. Moirai~2.0 uses \emph{multi-token prediction}, generating multiple future patches in a single pass rather than one step at a time. The decoding head outputs nine quantile levels (0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95) per forecast step. We use the \texttt{Salesforce/moirai-2.0-R-small} checkpoint and extract the median quantile as the point forecast.

\paragraph{Lag-Llama.}
Lag-Llama \citep{rasul2024lagllama} is a decoder-only foundation model based on the LLaMA architecture, adapted for univariate probabilistic time series forecasting. Unlike the patch-based models above, Lag-Llama tokenizes each time step individually using a set of \emph{lag features}: at each position $t$, the input vector consists of the current value and a fixed set of lagged values $(y_{t-1}, y_{t-2}, y_{t-7}, y_{t-14}, \ldots)$, concatenated with time-related covariates. This lag-based design is conceptually closer to an autoregressive econometric specification---the model explicitly receives lagged values as inputs---though the transformer learns the mapping from lags to forecasts nonparametrically rather than through a linear projection. The decoding head parameterizes a Student-$t$ mixture distribution at each forecast step: it outputs the location, scale, and degrees-of-freedom parameters for each mixture component. Forecasts are generated by sampling trajectories from this distribution and taking the median across samples. We use the pretrained checkpoint (8 layers, 4 attention heads, 36-dimensional embeddings per head), sample 20 trajectories, and report the median as the point forecast.

\paragraph{Kronos.}
Kronos \citep{kronos2024} uses \emph{kernel-based tokenization}: each continuous value is represented as a weighted combination of a set of learned kernel basis functions, producing a soft embedding rather than a hard bin assignment. The embedding is then processed by a T5 encoder-decoder transformer backbone. At decoding time, the model samples token-level outputs from the learned distribution and maps them back to continuous values through the inverse kernel transform. We use the \texttt{Salesforce/kronos-t5-tiny} checkpoint (approximately 8M parameters) with a sample count of 5 and take the median prediction as the point forecast.

\paragraph{Zero-shot protocol.}
All four TSFMs receive a rolling context window of 512 daily RV observations as input, with no additional features or covariates. For multi-step horizons ($h > 1$), the point forecast is the mean of the $h$-step output vector. No parameters are updated during the out-of-sample evaluation period; all forecasts are purely zero-shot. The 512-day context length is the default for these architectures, just as the 252-day rolling window is standard for HAR estimation. This reflects each approach's recommended configuration rather than an equal-information comparison; we view this as the practically relevant setup, since a practitioner would deploy each model with its standard settings. We extract the median rather than the mean of each model's predictive distribution because the median is the optimal point forecast under MAE loss and is more robust to the right-skewed forecast distributions that arise in volatility applications; for symmetric distributions, the two coincide. We note that the median is not the optimal point forecast under QLIKE loss (which is the conditional mean); our choice reflects a practical compromise between robustness and loss-function alignment. Table~\ref{tab:computational_cost} (Appendix) reports wall-clock timing for all models: econometric specifications require $< 0.01$\,s per forecast step on CPU, while TSFM inference ranges from 0.014\,s (Chronos-Bolt-Small on GPU) to ${\sim}5$\,s (Kronos on CPU).

\subsection{Forecast Evaluation}
\label{subsec:evaluation}

\paragraph{Walk-forward design.}
We employ a walk-forward evaluation scheme with a rolling origin and a fixed training window of 252 trading days (approximately one year). At each origin, the econometric model is estimated on the most recent 252 observations; the estimation window then slides forward one day at a time, producing daily re-estimated forecasts over a test block of 126 days (approximately six months). The origin then advances by 126 days and the process repeats until the end of the sample; the training window does not expand. For the equity sample (2,786 days), this produces approximately 2,408 out-of-sample forecasts per model--asset combination at $h = 1$; the FX and futures samples (4,240+ days) yield approximately 3,862. Foundation models follow the same walk-forward timing but require no estimation step; only the context window slides forward.

\paragraph{Forecast horizons.}
We evaluate three forecast horizons: $h = 1$ (one day), $h = 5$ (one week), and $h = 22$ (one month). The forecast target at horizon $h$ is the average realized volatility over the next $h$ days, $h^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^{h} RV_{t+i}$. All models produce direct $h$-step forecasts.

\paragraph{Loss functions.}
Our primary loss function is the quasi-likelihood (QLIKE) loss of \citet{patton2011}:
\begin{equation}
  L_{\text{QLIKE}}(\hat{\sigma}_t^2, RV_t) = \frac{RV_t}{\hat{\sigma}_t^2} - \log\frac{RV_t}{\hat{\sigma}_t^2} - 1,
  \label{eq:qlike}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\sigma}_t^2$ is the forecast and $RV_t$ is the realized value. \citet{patton2011} shows that QLIKE preserves the ranking of forecasts regardless of the choice of volatility proxy, making it the preferred metric when the true conditional variance is unobserved. We also report mean squared error (MSE), mean absolute error (MAE), and out-of-sample $R^2$ ($R^2_{\text{OOS}}$). All forecasts are floored at $10^{-6}$ to prevent numerical instability in the QLIKE calculation when models produce near-zero predictions.

\paragraph{Statistical tests.}
Pairwise forecast comparisons use the \citet{diebold1995} test with Newey--West variance estimation, applied to QLIKE loss differentials. To address the multiple comparison problem inherent in evaluating eleven models, we compute the Model Confidence Set (MCS) of \citet{hansen2011mcs}. The MCS identifies the subset of models whose forecasting ability cannot be statistically distinguished from the best model at a given significance level. We use the $T_{\max}$ test statistic with a block bootstrap (block length $= 22$ days, $B = 10{,}000$ replications) and significance level $\alpha = 0.10$.

\paragraph{Forecast efficiency.}
We assess forecast efficiency using the Mincer--Zarnowitz regression \citep{mincer1969}:
\begin{equation}
  RV_t = \alpha + \beta \, \hat{f}_t + \varepsilon_t,
  \label{eq:mz}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{f}_t$ is the model's forecast. Under forecast optimality, $\alpha = 0$ and $\beta = 1$---meaning the forecast is unbiased and captures the correct scale of variation. We test the joint null $H_0\!: \alpha = 0, \beta = 1$ using a Wald test with Newey--West standard errors. Deviations from $\beta = 1$ indicate systematic over- or undershoot; low $R^2$ indicates that the forecast explains little of the variation in realized values. We report cross-asset averages and the fraction of assets for which the joint null is rejected at the 5\% level.

\paragraph{Time-varying relative performance.}
The DM test and MCS measure average predictive ability over the full out-of-sample period. To examine whether relative forecast performance is stable over time, we apply the \citet{giacomini2010} fluctuation test. For each model paired against the HAR benchmark, we compute a rolling DM statistic over a window of size $m = \lfloor 0.3 \times T \rfloor$, producing a time path of relative QLIKE performance. The test statistic is $\sup_t |S_t|$, the supremum of the absolute rolling DM statistics, compared against critical values from the distribution of the supremum of a standardized Brownian bridge. Rejection of the null indicates that the two models' relative performance changes significantly over time---a result that the unconditional DM test would miss.

At the one-day horizon ($h = 1$), Log-HAR achieves the lowest average QLIKE (0.220), followed closely by Moirai~2.0 (0.226) and Chronos-Bolt-Small (0.229). The gap between Log-HAR and the best TSFM is small---roughly 3\% in relative terms---and is driven by the log-space formulation of Log-HAR, which guarantees positive forecasts and penalizes proportional forecast errors in a way that aligns with QLIKE's structure. On MSE, MAE, and $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$, however, the TSFMs dominate: Moirai~2.0 achieves the lowest MSE ($0.210 \times 10^{-6}$), and the highest $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ (0.426), compared to 0.238 for Log-HAR. Chronos-Bolt-Base achieves the lowest MAE ($1.17 \times 10^{-4}$). The out-of-sample $R^2$ gap between TSFMs and the best econometric model is 8--19 percentage points at $h = 1$, a substantial margin.

At the five-day horizon ($h = 5$), the TSFM advantage becomes more pronounced. Moirai~2.0 achieves the best score across all four metrics: MSE ($0.122 \times 10^{-6}$), MAE ($1.07 \times 10^{-4}$), QLIKE (0.188), and $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ (0.384). The gap over Log-HAR in QLIKE terms is now 19\%, compared to 3\% at $h = 1$. Chronos-Bolt-Small achieves the second-lowest QLIKE (0.213), still below Log-HAR's 0.231. The econometric models that do best at $h = 1$ (HAR, HAR-J) deteriorate more rapidly at longer horizons, while the TSFMs maintain relatively stable accuracy.

At the monthly horizon ($h = 22$), Moirai~2.0 again dominates: QLIKE = 0.257, MSE = $0.098 \times 10^{-6}$, and $R^2_{\text{OOS}} = 0.110$. The econometric benchmarks now show near-zero or negative $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$, indicating that they barely outperform (or underperform) the historical mean at this horizon. Log-HAR achieves $R^2_{\text{OOS}} = -0.044$, while Chronos-Bolt-Small maintains a positive $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ of 0.085. This horizon-dependent pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that TSFMs' large context windows (512 days) capture long-range dependencies more effectively than the HAR's fixed three-component structure.

Levels-based HAR extensions are unstable in this evaluation. HAR-RS and HARQ produce QLIKE values of 3.73 and 16.46 at $h = 1$, respectively, rising to 9.40 and 12.42 at $h = 22$. These inflated values arise because the OLS-estimated models occasionally produce near-zero forecasts for $RV_{t+h}$, which are floored at $10^{-6}$ for QLIKE evaluation. Across the full sample, no forecast is literally negative, but the floor binds for a small fraction of observations. Since $\text{QLIKE} = RV / \hat{RV} - \log(RV / \hat{RV}) - 1$, a forecast near zero generates an extremely large loss whenever the realized value is far from zero, and a handful of such observations can dominate the cross-sectional average. Across the equity sample, the $10^{-6}$ floor binds for fewer than 0.1\% of TSFM forecast--date pairs but for 2--5\% of HAR-RS and HARQ pairs, explaining their inflated QLIKE. Table~\ref{tab:main_results_median} (Appendix) reports cross-sectional medians, which are robust to these outliers; the qualitative ranking of models is unchanged. Log-HAR and ARFIMA avoid this problem entirely by operating in log space, where forecasts are positive by construction. TSFMs also produce strictly positive forecasts, since their training distributions are bounded below. This practical advantage may be as important as any differences in statistical accuracy: a model that occasionally produces degenerate forecasts is difficult to deploy in production risk systems.

Bootstrap confidence intervals (10,000 draws, percentile method) on the cross-sectional mean QLIKE confirm the statistical significance of the key rankings. At $h = 1$, the 95\% CI for Log-HAR QLIKE is $[0.204, 0.239]$, overlapping with Moirai~2.0's $[0.209, 0.247]$ and Chronos-Bolt-Small's $[0.211, 0.251]$---consistent with the MCS finding that these models are statistically indistinguishable. At $h = 5$, Moirai~2.0's CI $[0.172, 0.209]$ lies entirely below Log-HAR's $[0.208, 0.257]$, confirming the TSFM advantage at this horizon. At $h = 22$, Moirai~2.0 $[0.238, 0.277]$ and Lag-Llama $[0.260, 0.306]$ both separate cleanly from Log-HAR $[0.294, 0.417]$.

Three patterns emerge from the cross-asset comparison. Second, the relative ranking of TSFMs shifts across asset classes: Moirai~2.0 dominates equities (where persistence is high), while Chronos-Bolt performs better for FX and short-horizon futures (where dynamics are smoother or less persistent). \paragraph{Interpretation.}
The MCS, DM, MZ, and fluctuation test results jointly provide a multidimensional picture of relative forecast performance. The MCS and DM tests establish that the best zero-shot TSFMs have achieved statistical parity with the best econometric benchmarks. The MZ regressions reveal that TSFMs produce better-calibrated forecasts at long horizons ($\hat{\beta}$ closer to 1, higher $R^2$), while econometric models suffer severe undershoot at $h = 22$. The Giacomini--Rossi test shows that relative performance is not constant: Log-HAR's QLIKE advantage at $h = 1$ erodes over time, and TSFMs consistently outperform HAR from their entry into the sample onward.

At $h = 1$, Log-HAR remains the marginal leader on QLIKE, but Moirai~2.0 and Chronos-Bolt are statistically indistinguishable from it for most assets. At $h = 5$ and $h = 22$, Moirai~2.0 is the single model most consistently in the confidence set. However, the results also reveal substantial heterogeneity across TSFMs: Lag-Llama and Kronos are systematically excluded at short horizons, with Lag-Llama recovering only at $h = 22$ and Kronos performing poorly throughout. This heterogeneity suggests that architecture and pretraining data matter as much as the zero-shot paradigm itself. The practical implication is that TSFMs offer a viable alternative to HAR-family models, particularly for horizons beyond one day, but practitioners should evaluate multiple TSFM architectures rather than assuming uniform performance.

\subsection{Portfolio Application: Realized Covariance Forecasting}
\label{subsec:portfolio}

The preceding analysis focused on univariate realized variance forecasting. The GMV portfolio exercise serves as our primary economic significance test, translating forecast accuracy differences into realized portfolio risk. We now extend the evaluation to a multivariate setting by examining whether covariance forecasts from TSFMs can improve portfolio allocation. We construct global minimum variance (GMV) portfolios using four approaches to covariance forecasting and compare the resulting realized portfolio variance.

\paragraph{Covariance forecasting methods.}
We consider four covariance forecasting approaches. (i) \emph{Element-HAR}: a separate HAR model is fitted to each element of the realized covariance matrix, treating variances and covariances as independent univariate series. (ii) \emph{HAR-DRD}: the realized covariance matrix is decomposed as $\Sigma_t = D_t R_t D_t$, where $D_t$ is the diagonal matrix of realized volatilities and $R_t$ is the realized correlation matrix; HAR models are fitted separately to each element of $D_t$ and $R_t$, and the covariance forecast is reconstructed. This DRD decomposition ensures positive semi-definiteness by construction when the forecast correlation matrix is adjusted to be positive semi-definite (PSD). (iii--iv) \emph{Chronos-Bolt-Small} and \emph{Moirai~2.0}: zero-shot element-wise TSFM forecasts applied to each covariance pair, followed by PSD projection. We also include the $1/N$ equal-weight portfolio as a naive benchmark.

\paragraph{Forex GMV results.}
Table~\ref{tab:portfolio} reports the average realized variance of GMV portfolios constructed from each forecasting model, along with portfolio turnover and concentration metrics. For the 5-asset FX portfolio, all model-based approaches achieve substantial variance reduction relative to $1/N$. At $h = 1$, Chronos-Bolt-Small achieves the lowest average realized variance ($3.01 \times 10^{-6}$), a 44.9\% reduction relative to $1/N$ ($5.46 \times 10^{-6}$). HAR-DRD is close behind ($3.11 \times 10^{-6}$, 43.0\% reduction), while Element-HAR ($3.52 \times 10^{-6}$, 35.5\%) and Moirai~2.0 ($3.55 \times 10^{-6}$, 35.0\%) are somewhat less effective. At $h = 5$, Chronos-Bolt and HAR-DRD maintain their advantage (45.5\% and 44.7\% reduction, respectively), while Moirai~2.0 produces an unstable covariance forecast that results in higher realized variance than $1/N$. At $h = 22$, all methods except Moirai~2.0 produce comparable variance reductions (31--39\%).

\paragraph{Futures GMV results.}
For the 5-contract futures portfolio (Table~\ref{tab:portfolio}), Chronos-Bolt-Small achieves the lowest realized variance at $h = 1$ ($3.36 \times 10^{-5}$, a 68.8\% reduction vs.\ $1/N$) and $h = 5$ ($3.55 \times 10^{-5}$, 67.2\%). Moirai~2.0 performs worse, particularly at $h = 5$, where its realized variance exceeds $1/N$ by a factor of 3.5. This failure stems from Moirai's element-wise covariance forecasts being internally inconsistent: the assembled matrices are not positive semi-definite, and the eigenvalue floor used in PSD projection ($10^{-10}$) is too small relative to the matrix scale (typical eigenvalues $\sim 10^{-5}$). The resulting near-singular matrices produce extreme portfolio weights on a handful of dates, which dominate the average realized variance. Chronos-Bolt does not suffer this problem because its forecasts produce matrices with minimum eigenvalues around $10^{-6}$, keeping condition numbers below 10.

\paragraph{Stocks GMV results.}
For the 40-stock equity portfolio (Table~\ref{tab:portfolio}), the dimensionality problem becomes acute. Only HAR-DRD produces sensible portfolios, achieving a realized variance of $5.11 \times 10^{-5}$ at $h = 1$---a 29.4\% reduction relative to $1/N$ ($7.24 \times 10^{-5}$). All element-wise approaches (Element-HAR, Chronos-Bolt, Moirai~2.0) fail catastrophically: their realized variances exceed $1/N$ by two to three orders of magnitude, with average daily turnover above 7 (Chronos-Bolt) and 50 (Moirai). The problem is that element-wise forecasting of 820 unique covariance pairs produces assembled matrices with extremely small eigenvalues. PSD projection with a floor of $10^{-10}$ yields near-singular matrices whose inverse concentrates portfolio weight on a few assets. The DRD decomposition avoids this by separately modeling volatilities and correlations, ensuring that the reconstructed matrix inherits the PSD structure of the correlation forecast. This result highlights a practical limitation: element-wise TSFM covariance forecasting does not scale to high-dimensional portfolios without structural constraints. To verify that this failure is not an artifact of the eigenvalue floor, we re-projected all stocks forecast matrices using a floor of $10^{-6}$ (six orders of magnitude larger than the default $10^{-10}$) and re-evaluated the GMV portfolios. The higher floor reduces turnover substantially---from 13.7 to 2.2 for Chronos-Bolt and from 52.7 to 7.2 for Moirai at $h = 1$---but realized variances remain 5--7 times above $1/N$ ($3.6 \times 10^{-4}$ and $5.0 \times 10^{-4}$ vs.\ $7.2 \times 10^{-5}$). HAR-DRD continues to outperform $1/N$ ($4.6 \times 10^{-5}$). The failure of element-wise approaches thus reflects a fundamental structural problem, not a tuning parameter.

\paragraph{Turnover.}
The $1/N$ portfolio has zero turnover by construction. Among model-based portfolios in the low-dimensional FX and futures settings, HAR-DRD achieves the lowest turnover (0.080 for FX at $h = 1$), followed by Moirai~2.0 (0.060) and Chronos-Bolt (0.116). The DRD decomposition's smoothness advantage comes from its separate modeling of volatilities and correlations, which dampens spurious variation in off-diagonal elements.

\paragraph{Covariance forecast accuracy.}
Table~\ref{tab:cov_accuracy} reports element-wise forecast accuracy across all three asset classes. Two metrics are computed: the normalized Frobenius norm of the forecast error matrix ($\|\hat{\Sigma}_t - \Sigma_t\|_F / N$) and the QLIKE loss on diagonal (variance) elements. The results show that TSFMs produce competitive covariance forecasts at the element level, even when the assembled matrices are ill-conditioned for portfolio optimization. For FX at $h = 1$, Chronos-Bolt achieves the lowest Frobenius norm ($0.86 \times 10^{-5}$) and near-best diagonal QLIKE (0.145 vs.\ 0.133 for HAR-DRD). For futures, Chronos-Bolt and HAR-DRD are comparable on Frobenius norm, with HAR-DRD leading on QLIKE at $h = 5$ and $h = 22$. For stocks, Chronos-Bolt achieves the lowest Frobenius norm at $h = 1$ and $h = 5$, while HAR-DRD leads on diagonal QLIKE. Moirai~2.0 is competitive on stocks (QLIKE 0.175 at $h = 1$, matching Chronos-Bolt) but weaker on FX (QLIKE 0.308). The disconnect between element-wise accuracy and portfolio performance---Chronos-Bolt's Frobenius norm is lower than HAR-DRD's for stocks, yet its portfolios fail---confirms that the problem lies in the spectral properties of the assembled matrix, not in the accuracy of individual element forecasts.

\section{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:robustness}
% ============================================================

We assess the stability of our main findings along several dimensions.

\paragraph{Cross-dataset validation.}
As a supplementary check, we replicated the univariate forecasting exercise on the CAPIRe dataset (29 DJIA stocks, 2003--2024, annualized percentage units). Model rankings are consistent with VOLARE; results are available from the authors upon request.

\paragraph{Sub-sample stability: pre- and post-COVID.}
Table~\ref{tab:subsample} splits the equity sample at March 1, 2020, and reports forecast accuracy separately for the pre-COVID (2015--2020) and post-COVID (2020--2026) periods. The sub-sample analysis reveals that TSFM performance is regime-robust, while levels-based econometric models are not.

In the pre-COVID period, Log-HAR achieves the lowest QLIKE at all horizons (0.206 at $h=1$, 0.182 at $h=5$, 0.321 at $h=22$), though Chronos-Bolt-Base matches it on MSE and $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ at $h=1$. The pre-COVID period features lower average volatility and fewer extreme events, conditions that favor the parsimonious HAR specification.

In the post-COVID period, TSFMs dominate across all metrics and horizons. Moirai~2.0 achieves the lowest QLIKE at $h=1$ (0.213 vs.\ 0.225 for Log-HAR), $h=5$ (0.174 vs.\ 0.252), and $h=22$ (0.168 vs.\ 0.335), while also achieving the highest $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ (0.430, 0.378, 0.148). The levels-based HAR variants suffer extreme QLIKE inflation in the high-volatility regime: HARQ's QLIKE rises from 2.35 pre-COVID to 23.63 post-COVID at $h=1$, because the near-zero forecast problem is exacerbated when realized values are large. Log-HAR is more stable across regimes than its levels-based counterparts but still falls behind the TSFMs on all metrics in the post-COVID sample. The TSFM heterogeneity pattern persists across sub-samples: Lag-Llama underperforms in both periods at $h = 1$ (QLIKE 0.433 pre-COVID, 0.385 post-COVID) but improves at longer horizons, while Kronos achieves the lowest MSE pre-COVID at $h = 1$ (0.082) but has inflated QLIKE in both periods due to systematic forecast bias. These results suggest that TSFMs' advantage is concentrated in volatile market conditions, where their flexible nonparametric forecasts adapt more effectively than the HAR's linear structure.

\input{tables/table_subsample}

\paragraph{Estimation window: 252 vs.\ 512 days.}
A potential concern is that TSFMs receive 512 days of context while econometric models use 252-day estimation windows, creating an information asymmetry that may favor TSFMs. To address this, we re-estimate all six econometric benchmarks with a 512-day rolling window, matching the TSFM context length. Table~\ref{tab:window_512} (Appendix) reports the results at $h = 1$ for the 40-equity sample. Five of six models improve under the longer window: HAR $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ rises from 0.340 to 0.435, and levels-HAR QLIKE inflation decreases substantially (e.g., HAR-RS QLIKE drops from 3.48 to 0.57). The exception is ARFIMA, whose long-memory estimation degrades with a 512-day window ($R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ falls from 0.132 to $-0.332$). Cross-model $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ rankings are highly stable (Spearman $\rho = 0.94$, $p < 0.01$). Importantly, even after granting econometric models the same 512-day information set, Chronos-Bolt and Moirai zero-shot QLIKE (0.19--0.23; Table~\ref{tab:main_results}) remain competitive with or below the best 512-day econometric QLIKE (Log-HAR at 0.20), confirming that the TSFM advantage is not an artifact of the longer context window.

\paragraph{QLIKE floor sensitivity.}
Our QLIKE implementation floors forecasts at $10^{-10}$ to prevent division by zero. Because levels-based HAR variants occasionally produce near-zero forecasts, this floor choice could affect model rankings. Table~\ref{tab:qlike_floor_sensitivity} (Appendix) reports cross-asset mean QLIKE at $h = 1$ under four floor values ($10^{-4}$, $10^{-6}$, $10^{-8}$, $10^{-10}$). QLIKE values are identical for floors $\leq 10^{-6}$ (Spearman rank correlation $\rho = 1.0$ vs.\ $10^{-10}$), confirming that the floor never binds at these levels. Only the aggressive $10^{-4}$ floor alters rankings ($\rho = 0.60$) by clipping levels-HAR forecasts that drive QLIKE inflation (e.g., HARQ drops from 16.04 to 0.38). Our default $10^{-10}$ floor is conservative and does not distort model comparisons.

\paragraph{MZ bias-corrected evaluation.}
TSFMs may achieve low loss through forecast calibration rather than genuine predictive ability. To test this, we apply a recursive Mincer--Zarnowitz bias correction to all TSFM forecasts at $h = 1$: at each time $t$, we estimate $\hat{\alpha}_t$ and $\hat{\beta}_t$ from all observations up to $t - 1$, then form the corrected forecast $\hat{f}_t^{\text{MZ}} = \hat{\alpha}_t + \hat{\beta}_t f_t$ using an expanding estimation window (minimum 252 days). Table~\ref{tab:mz_bias_corrected} (Appendix) compares original and MZ-corrected TSFM forecasts against econometric benchmarks, evaluated on the common post-warm-up sample. MZ correction improves QLIKE for the well-calibrated Chronos-Bolt models (Chronos-Bolt-Base: 0.2307 $\to$ 0.1918), but worsens QLIKE for models with systematic bias (Lag-Llama, Kronos) where the linear correction pushes some forecasts toward zero. MSE and $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ are largely unchanged, confirming that TSFM accuracy reflects genuine predictive content rather than calibration artifacts.

\paragraph{Remaining directions.}
VOLARE provides alternative realized measures including subsampled realized variance ($RV^{(5)}_{\text{SS}}$), realized kernels, and range-based estimators. Evaluating model rankings under alternative volatility proxies and varying TSFM context lengths (128 to 512 days) are directions for future work that would further establish the generality of our findings.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper evaluates zero-shot time series foundation models for realized volatility forecasting across 50 assets spanning U.S.\ equities, foreign exchange, and futures. We compare four TSFMs---Chronos-Bolt, Moirai~2.0, Lag-Llama, and Kronos---against six econometric specifications at three forecast horizons using the VOLARE dataset, with formal statistical testing via Diebold--Mariano tests and Model Confidence Sets.

Three main findings emerge. First, the best zero-shot TSFMs are competitive with the best econometric benchmarks. This result differs from prior work on daily returns \citep{rahimikia2025revisiting} and on TimesFM~1.0 \citep{goel2025rv}, and is consistent with the hypothesis that realized volatility's positive, persistent, mean-reverting dynamics align more closely with the macroeconomic and physical time series on which TSFMs were pretrained. However, not all TSFMs perform equally: Kronos is excluded from the MCS for virtually all assets at all horizons, and Lag-Llama is excluded at short horizons ($h = 1$, $h = 5$) but enters for 95\% of assets at $h = 22$. Second, the ranking of models depends on the loss function and forecast horizon. Log-HAR achieves the lowest QLIKE at $h = 1$ for equities, but TSFMs dominate on MSE, MAE, and $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ at all horizons. At $h = 5$ and $h = 22$, Moirai~2.0 achieves the lowest QLIKE as well. Model Confidence Set analysis shows that Moirai~2.0 enters the MCS for 100\% of assets at $h = 5$ and 97.5\% at $h = 22$, while Log-HAR enters for 90\% at $h = 1$. Third, model rankings are not stable across asset classes: Chronos-Bolt outperforms on FX and futures, while Moirai~2.0 dominates equities.

A practical finding is that levels-based HAR extensions (HAR-RS, HARQ) produce near-zero forecasts that inflate QLIKE by orders of magnitude, a problem that log-space models and TSFMs avoid. Sub-sample analysis reveals that TSFM advantages are concentrated in the volatile post-COVID period (2020--2026), where Moirai~2.0 dominates all metrics and horizons, while Log-HAR retains a QLIKE edge in the calmer pre-COVID period. For portfolio construction, element-wise zero-shot TSFM forecasts of realized covariance matrices achieve substantial variance reductions (35--69\%) relative to $1/N$ in low-dimensional GMV portfolios (5 assets), with Chronos-Bolt performing comparably to HAR-DRD baselines. However, element-wise TSFM covariance forecasting fails for the 40-stock portfolio, where only the structurally constrained HAR-DRD approach produces viable allocations---despite TSFMs achieving competitive element-wise forecast accuracy. The GMV portfolio results provide economic significance: Chronos-Bolt reduces annualized portfolio variance by 45--69\% relative to equal-weight allocation for FX and futures, demonstrating that TSFM forecast accuracy gains translate into meaningful risk reduction.

Several limitations should be noted. We evaluate only zero-shot TSFM performance; fine-tuning on realized volatility data may yield further gains. Our evaluation is restricted to point forecasts; density forecast evaluation and interval calibration \citep{adler2025calibration} remain open questions. We use a single volatility estimator (5-minute realized variance); sensitivity to the choice of estimator is left for future work. The covariance forecasting exercise uses a simple element-wise approach that fails at high dimensions; natively multivariate TSFM architectures \citep{ansari2025chronos2} or structural decompositions applied to TSFM outputs may address this limitation.

Future work should pursue four directions. First, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (e.g., LoRA for Chronos-2, epoch-based training for Lag-Llama) on realized volatility data may close the remaining gap with Log-HAR on QLIKE at short horizons; our preliminary Lag-Llama fine-tuning results in Section~\ref{sec:robustness} suggest that adaptation improves performance at short horizons. Second, density forecast evaluation would test whether TSFMs provide well-calibrated prediction intervals, a property required for Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall applications. Third, natively multivariate TSFMs could be applied directly to realized covariance matrices, potentially outperforming the element-wise approach used here. Fourth, extending the evaluation to emerging markets and alternative asset classes would test the generality of our findings.

The broader implication of our results is that the best zero-shot time series foundation models have reached a level of accuracy that makes them a practical tool for volatility forecasting. For practitioners, Moirai~2.0 and Chronos-Bolt combine competitive accuracy with zero estimation overhead and immediate deployment to new assets---advantages that become increasingly valuable as the number of assets to be forecast grows. At the same time, the poor performance of Lag-Llama and Kronos at short horizons, and the failure of element-wise TSFM covariance forecasting at high dimensions, caution against treating TSFMs as a universal solution. Model selection and structural constraints remain essential.
